Collateral management

The margin-terms blind spot: what the 2024 CSA cycle revealed

An institutional analysis for collateral managers, derivatives treasury, and legal operations teams in capital markets

Q3 202611 MIN

For eleven and a half months in 2024, nobody in the European derivatives market could say with certainty how single-stock and index options would be margined the following year. Article 38 of the EU's bilateral margin technical standards had given equity options a derogation from initial margin requirements — but that derogation expired on 4 January 2024, and the extension meant to replace it was still working its way through the European Supervisory Authorities, the Commission, and Parliament when the clock ran out. It was not resolved until Regulation (EU) 2024/2987 — EMIR 3 — entered into force on 24 December 2024, making the exemption permanent, subject to review every three years.123

That gap is a small, dateable episode. But it forced a much larger and more uncomfortable question onto derivatives documentation and collateral teams across the market: for every counterparty relationship with equity options exposure, did they actually know — without opening individual agreements — which credit support annexes covered it, what the threshold and minimum transfer amount elections were, and whether an IM annex existed or would need to be built from scratch if the exemption lapsed? For a large number of firms, the honest answer was that nobody could pull that list centrally. It lived in individual negotiated documents, filed by counterparty, read one at a time.

What a CSA actually controls, and why it goes stale

A Credit Support Annex is the collateral engine bolted onto an ISDA Master Agreement. It does not decide whether two parties trade; it decides what happens to the money once they have. Paragraph 13 of the CSA — the part that gets negotiated line by line — sets the Threshold (the amount of uncollateralized exposure each side tolerates before a margin call can be made), the Minimum Transfer Amount or MTA (the smallest amount worth actually moving), the eligible collateral schedule, and the haircuts applied to each collateral type.45

None of those elections are static by nature, even though they are almost always treated as static in practice. A Threshold negotiated against one counterparty's credit profile in 2018 does not automatically stay appropriate as that counterparty's rating, balance sheet, or trading volume changes. A haircut schedule agreed at execution reflects the volatility assumptions of that moment; it is not revisited when market volatility regimes shift. Clarus Financial Technology's plain description of CSA mechanics makes the point directly: haircut levels are set when a CSA is negotiated and are not adjusted in line with changes in the market — full stop. The document is a snapshot. The market it was written for keeps moving.4

Regulatory minimum transfer amounts add a further layer that firms sometimes forget is a ceiling, not a target: under EU and UK bilateral margin rules, the MTA for the combined exchange of initial and variation margin cannot exceed €500,000 (or the local-currency equivalent) at the legal-entity level. Individual CSAs can and do set lower figures, and many were negotiated years before a given counterparty relationship carried the notional it carries today — meaning the operative constraint on a given trading desk's actual liquidity exposure is often not the regulatory ceiling but whatever number sits in a document nobody has reopened since it was signed.5

The back book nobody re-read: 2017 to 2024

To understand why so few firms could answer the portfolio-wide question in 2024, it helps to know when most of the current CSA population was actually written. The answer, for a large share of the market, is March 2017.

The variation margin "Big Bang" — the compliance deadline for the new VM rules under EMIR and equivalent regimes globally — required a colossal repapering exercise compressed into a matter of weeks. Final national rules in some jurisdictions were published only months before the deadline, leaving almost no runway. By the end of February 2017, only around a third of the required CSA amendments across the market had been completed; adoption did not reach roughly 90% until the week ending 11 August 2017. Clarus FT's contemporaneous analysis described the scale plainly: a roughly 60-percentage-point jump in completion over that stretch represented tens of thousands of newly amended CSAs, each the product of a bilateral negotiation.6

That wave set the terms — thresholds, MTAs, eligible collateral, haircut schedules — that a large portion of the market has been operating under ever since. Firms that adhered via the ISDA 2016 Variation Margin Protocol got a compliant document quickly; they did not get a document tailored to how their trading relationships would evolve over the following seven years. Amending a CSA after the fact is neither free nor mechanical — changing Paragraph 13 terms on a live agreement can move the value of the collateralized portfolio itself, which is exactly the kind of consequence that makes counterparties reluctant to reopen the conversation absent a forcing event. So mostly, nobody did.7

The 2024 equity options deadline was one of the few forcing events sizeable enough to make firms look again — not because it required amending every CSA in the book, but because it required firms to determine which agreements were even relevant to the question, and that determination turned out to be the hard part.

The visibility problem, not the negotiation problem

Ask a collateral manager how many active CSAs their firm holds and most can answer within a rounding error. Ask which of those CSAs have a Threshold above a given number, or which ones still reference haircut schedules that predate a specific market stress, and the honest answer in a large share of firms is a multi-week data-gathering exercise rather than a query.

The industry's own tooling responses point to how widespread this gap is. AcadiaSoft — whose Hub is used by more than 650 firms exchanging on the order of $400 billion of collateral daily — markets its Collateral Manager and CSA Valuation services explicitly around building "a single, golden source document repository for all collateral agreements," language that only makes commercial sense if the default state most firms are moving away from is a fragmented one. Its separate IM Threshold Monitor service exists specifically because Phase 4 and 5 in-scope firms needed a way to compare live IM exposure against their estimated threshold — a comparison that requires knowing the threshold in the first place, portfolio-wide, not counterparty by counterparty.89

ISDA's own infrastructure work tells the same story from the standards side. Its Common Domain Model initiative for collateral is explicitly aimed at digitizing CSA and Eligible Collateral Schedule terms so that agreement data can be consumed programmatically "with limited manual processing," reducing the operational risk created when non-standard terms require manual review by operations staff before an agreement can even be executed. That framing — non-standard terms requiring manual operational review — describes a market where the default state of CSA data is still human-readable prose, not structured, queryable fields.10

None of this is a negotiation failure. Every one of those CSAs was, individually, negotiated correctly at the time it was signed. The failure is architectural: no firm built a system that kept the aggregate picture current as the underlying documents individually drifted out of date, one bilateral amendment or one unrevisited threshold at a time.

What it costs when volatility returns

The consequence of not knowing your aggregate margin-call exposure is not abstract, and 2022 supplied the case study.

In the days following the UK's September 2022 "mini-budget," the 30-year gilt yield rose by roughly 160 basis points in four trading days — a move without precedent in scope and speed. Liability-driven investment funds running leveraged interest-rate hedges were hit with margin calls they had not sized their liquidity for. Because those funds needed cash fast and their underlying assets were long-dated gilts, the result was forced selling into an already falling market — a feedback loop between collateral calls and asset prices that the Bank of England's own working paper on the episode helped formalize as a case study in why margin and collateral obligations, in aggregate, are a liquidity risk factor and not just a bilateral credit-risk mechanic.1112

The post-mortem response was not "renegotiate the CSAs." It was liquidity-side: the FCA's subsequent guidance pushed LDI managers toward capital buffers "well above" regulatory minimums and waterfall arrangements giving fast access to liquid assets when further collateral is required. That is a sensible fix for the specific failure mode observed in gilts. But it treats the symptom — insufficient liquidity buffer — without addressing the underlying visibility problem: a fund, or a bank's trading book, cannot correctly size a liquidity buffer against a set of threshold and MTA obligations it cannot see in aggregate.13

The Financial Stability Board picked up that thread directly in 2024. Its consultation report on Liquidity Preparedness for Margin and Collateral Calls, published in April 2024 and finalized in December 2024 after twenty-five responses from trade bodies spanning banking, asset management, insurance, and hedge funds, sets out recommendations aimed squarely at reducing the procyclical behaviour that amplified the 2022 gilt stress. Its core asks — liquidity stress testing calibrated to margin and collateral call scenarios, and governance sufficient to ensure collateral is available when required — are not exercises a firm can perform meaningfully without a current, portfolio-wide inventory of what its actual threshold and MTA exposures are. A stress test built on stale assumptions about your own documentation produces a false sense of preparedness, which may be worse than no test at all.1415

Scale makes the stakes concrete. ISDA's Margin Survey for year-end 2024 found that the leading derivatives market participants it surveyed collected $1.5 trillion in initial and variation margin combined — $431.2 billion of IM and just over $1.0 trillion of VM, the latter up 9.3% on the year before. That is the flow moving through a documentation architecture that, for a large share of the underlying agreements, was last actively reviewed under 2017 market conditions.16

The monitoring architecture that closes the gap

The fix that survives contact with an examiner, or with the next volatility spike, is not a one-time inventory exercise. A spreadsheet snapshot of every CSA's Threshold and MTA is stale the day after it is compiled, for the same reason the original documents went stale: nothing keeps it synchronized with subsequent amendments, counterparty credit changes, or new agreements.

What holds up instead is treating the CSA population the same way a well-run legal-operations function treats any other high-volume contract category: as a structured, continuously maintained dataset rather than a filing cabinet consulted on demand. In practice that means four things working together, mirroring the operational pattern ISDA's own Suggested Operational Practices for the OTC Derivatives Collateral Process lay out for the adjacent problem of margin call disputes — proactive identification before a call goes out, not reactive scrambling after.17

A structured extraction layer across the existing book. Every live CSA's Threshold, MTA, eligible collateral schedule, and haircut terms pulled into a queryable format — not read one document at a time when a question comes up, but extracted once and kept current. This is precisely the gap ISDA's CDM work for collateral and AcadiaSoft's golden-source framing are both aimed at closing, from opposite ends of the standards-versus-tooling spectrum.108

Threshold and exposure monitoring that runs continuously, not on demand. The same logic behind AcadiaSoft's IM Threshold Monitor — comparing live exposure against the documented threshold on an ongoing basis — is equally applicable to the legacy VM-only population that most firms still treat as fixed. A threshold that made sense against a counterparty's 2018 credit profile is a live risk if nobody is checking whether that profile still holds.9

Event-triggered review, not just an annual cycle. A counterparty credit downgrade, a new product line trading with an existing relationship, a regulatory deadline like Article 38's expiry — each of these should trigger a targeted review of the specific CSAs affected, rather than waiting for whatever periodic review cadence a firm happens to run. The 2024 equity options episode is itself the clearest illustration: firms that could immediately generate the list of counterparties with equity options exposure and cross-reference it against existing IM documentation status spent that year in a materially different position than firms that had to build that list by hand under deadline pressure.

Liquidity stress testing built on the actual documented terms, not assumed ones. The FSB's 2024 recommendations only produce a meaningful answer if the threshold and MTA figures feeding the stress scenario are the real, current ones — which returns the exercise to the same structured-data prerequisite as everything above it.14

None of this requires renegotiating the underlying commercial terms. A Threshold that no longer fits a relationship may still be the right number to leave in place; the point is that it should be a decision, made with visibility, rather than a default inherited silently from 2017.

The closing argument

The 2024 CSA cycle did not produce a single dramatic failure the way the 2022 gilt crisis did. It produced something quieter and, in a way, more instructive: a full year in which the market operated under genuine regulatory uncertainty about a collateral obligation that ultimately resolved without incident, and in doing so exposed how few firms could answer a basic question about their own documentation without a manual search.

That is the actual lesson of the cycle, and it will recur. EMIR 3's equity options exemption is reviewable every three years, not permanent in the sense of never being revisited again. The next forcing event — a new margin phase-in, a benchmark change, a counterparty credit event — will again ask firms to produce, quickly, a portfolio-wide answer about what their CSAs actually say. The firms that can generate that answer as a query rather than a project will spend the next cycle managing risk. The rest will spend it finding out what they signed.
