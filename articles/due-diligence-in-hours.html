Deal advisory

Due diligence in hours, not weeks: rebuilding the deal-review stack

Written for deal counsel, M&A partners, and corporate development leaders

Q1 202614 MIN

The Time Problem Is Getting Worse

The data room opens. So does the clock.

In a competitive process, the buyer with the sharpest, fastest diligence can move from LOI to clear risk picture before a rival's team has finished indexing the folder structure. That advantage is not theoretical — it is structural. And the structural pressure is intensifying.

The average due diligence period has grown 64% over the past decade, rising from 124 days in 2014 to 203 days today. For private-target deals specifically — which are the majority of the market — that average stretches to 234 days. Bayes Business School's M&A Research Centre, working with SS&C Intralinks across more than 900 global transactions announced between 2013 and 2023, documented this trend systematically and also found something counterintuitive: the longest processes do not produce the best outcomes. Deals with medium-length due diligence periods of roughly 139 days had the highest completion rates, closed with lower acquisition premiums (averaging 22% versus 30% for short processes and 33% for long ones), and delivered 4% higher 12-month shareholder returns compared to processes that ran either too short or too long.1234

The implication is uncomfortable. Diligence is expanding — but expanding it beyond a certain point destroys value rather than protecting it. The profession is not doing its best work at 234 days. It is doing its most expensive, most exhausting, and often least focused work.

Meanwhile, SRS Acquiom and Mergermarket, in their 2026 survey of 150 senior U.S. investment banking executives, found that 73% expect the M&A due diligence process to become more complex over the next 12 to 24 months. One in five respondents reported that timelines had already extended over the past two years, with 57% of that group noting one to three months of added process. KPMG's 2025 M&A Deal Market Study, drawing on 300 dealmakers, identified completing due diligence as one of the top three obstacles to closing, cited by 41% of respondents — tied with navigating regulatory hurdles.56

And BCG analysis found that 40% of announced deals exceed their expected closing timelines. The leading driver of those overruns is not regulatory review. It is the document review process itself.7

Where the Hours Actually Go

A mid-market private-target acquisition typically lands 3,600 to 4,000 files in the data room. A larger deal can exceed 7,500 files. Bloomberg Law's standard M&A due diligence request list contains 174 document types across 10 categories, but the actual page volume for a mid-market deal runs from 5,000 to 50,000 pages — and in large-cap or cross-border transactions, it can exceed one million pages.8910211

Research consistently shows that deal teams spend more than 60% of their total diligence time on document review and extraction tasks — not on analysis, not on judgment calls, not on negotiating risk allocation. That means the highest-paid professionals on a deal team are spending the majority of their billable hours doing work that is more accurately described as search and categorization. A mid-market transaction involving 300 to 500 documents can consume two to four weeks of pure document grinding before substantive analysis begins.12

This is not a discipline problem. It is a structural one. The work falls into three distinct cost centers:

Initial triage and classification. In a 14,000-document data room, just the initial assessment of what exists and what matters can consume 300 to 400 billable hours. Documents arrive mislabeled, in inconsistent formats, with no hierarchy that maps to the diligence checklist. A contract that should be in "Material Agreements" is filed under "Miscellaneous." An amendment with an embedded assignment restriction is nested inside a parent file with a generic name.13

Clause-level extraction across a document population. This is where the most critical exposure lives, and where the process fails most predictably. Change-of-control provisions, assignment restrictions, exclusivity obligations, indemnification caps, and termination triggers do not appear in the same location or in the same language across different agreements. A change-of-control clause may appear as a standalone defined term in one contract, as an implicit trigger tied to a "merger" definition in another, and embedded in a termination-for-convenience provision in a third. Reviewing each contract sequentially — the default approach — means the attorney reviewing contract 200 may not remember that contract 47 used language that should be compared. Cross-document pattern recognition is structurally absent from linear review.1415

Cross-referencing obligations across the full document set. A master services agreement references exhibits. Those exhibits reference schedules. The schedules may have been amended by side letters executed months or years after the original. Identifying which version of a term governs, and whether the amendment was captured in the data room at all, requires active tracking that a linear read-through does not support.

The consequence of missing the right clause in the right agreement is not abstract. In one documented mid-market acquisition, an AI-assisted extraction of change-of-control provisions across 180 customer contracts revealed that 23 contained consent requirements — and that 4 of those contracts represented 31% of the target's annual revenue. That finding materially changed the deal structure. The same finding in a manual sequential review has a reasonable probability of never surfacing at all, not because the reviewing attorney is incompetent, but because the standard review workflow does not build in systematic comparison across 180 agreements.13

For associates, peak diligence periods translate to reviewed documents at a pace that invites error. A legal team reviewing 50,000 documents over a standard 90-day window faces approximately 555 documents per day with no days off. Fatigue degrades pattern recognition. The last 200 documents in a day get worse analysis than the first 20. This is not a personnel problem any amount of staffing can fully solve — it is a process architecture problem.16

The Redesign Pattern: Structured Extraction + Risk-Based Routing

The firms moving fastest on this problem are not simply deploying faster tools. They are redesigning the review stack itself. The distinction matters because faster tools applied to a broken workflow produce faster wrong answers. The redesign has three interdependent components.

Build the Issue Framework Before the Data Room Opens

The diligence checklist is not the issue framework. The checklist tells you what documents to request. The issue framework tells you what to find once you have them — and at what severity threshold each finding triggers escalation, renegotiation, or deal-kill analysis.

An issue-spotting framework for contract review is a structured extraction template applied uniformly across the document set, keyed to the specific risk profile of the transaction. For a deal where the buyer intends to consolidate vendor relationships post-close, supplier exclusivity provisions need to be extracted and rated systematically — not flagged when an associate happens to notice one. For a deal where revenue concentration in the top three customers exceeds 60%, change-of-control consent requirements in customer contracts are a potential dealbreaker, not a checkbox item. The issue framework encodes that prioritization in advance, before the first document is opened.

This changes the nature of review. Instead of an associate reading each agreement and applying judgment about what matters, the review is structured around a set of pre-defined extraction targets — change-of-control triggers, assignment restrictions, exclusivity obligations, indemnification caps and carve-outs, auto-renewal terms, termination-for-cause versus convenience — applied consistently across every document in the relevant population. The result is a structured dataset rather than a collection of individual summaries.

The value of that dataset is in the comparison it enables. When 95% of customer contracts have a 30-day payment term but five have 90-day terms, that deviation is visible in a structured extract in a way it is not in 180 separate summaries. When the target has 23 contracts requiring change-of-control consent and 4 of them represent a third of revenue, the risk concentration is immediately quantifiable rather than buried in individual contract memos.13

Classify and Route by Risk, Not by Document Order

Sequential document review — starting at the top of the data room index and working down — is the single most expensive and least effective approach available. It treats a board resolution and a key customer contract as equally urgent because they appear in adjacent rows on the index. They are not.

Risk-based routing allocates reviewer time by anticipated risk density. High-risk document categories — material contracts, employment agreements for key personnel, IP assignments, leases with change-of-control provisions, regulatory correspondence — go to senior associates on day one. Boilerplate document types go to junior reviewers or, increasingly, to AI-assisted first pass with attorney validation. Documents that are likely duplicates, clearly irrelevant, or standard-form instruments that require no negotiation analysis are processed last or not at all.

This requires the classification to happen before substantive review begins. AI document classification tools, applied across the full data room population at the outset, can sort a 4,000-file data room by document type, flag anomalies — missing pages, outlier provisions, agreements that appear in the data room but have no corresponding executed version — and surface the subset of documents that warrant immediate senior attention. Classification accuracy for well-trained models runs between 93% and 97% on standard document types. The residual error rate does not eliminate the need for attorney review; it defines where attorney review should be concentrated.171813

Build the Extraction Layer Across the Full Population Before Drafting Summaries

The sequence that has historically prevailed in due diligence is: read contract → draft summary → move to next contract. The redesign inverts the middle step. Before individual contract summaries are drafted, the extraction layer runs across the full population. Change-of-control provisions are pulled from every relevant contract simultaneously. Assignment restrictions are extracted and compared. Indemnification caps are identified, normalized, and arrayed.

This produces an intermediate dataset — a cross-document term sheet, in effect — before any summary document is written. The attorney reviewing the extraction for customer contracts knows, before drafting a single memo section, which contracts have consent requirements, which have auto-termination language, and which contain provisions that deviate from the norm across the population. Summary drafting, at that point, is synthesis rather than discovery. The highest-value work — identifying what the extraction means for this deal — is performed by experienced attorneys operating with full visibility into the population-level pattern.

At Bird & Bird, using Luminance's AI on a 200,000-document employment contract review — a review spanning approximately 20,000 employees for a sell-side transaction — the team processed material that would previously have required 10% sampling due to time constraints. Sampling is exactly the kind of quiet risk that structured extraction eliminates: it is risk coverage by omission. By eliminating sampling, the firm delivered a complete review that improved the target's valuation.11

At Vieira de Almeida (VdA), a Portuguese firm engaged to review over 2,500 documents within a five-day deadline for a real estate asset-backed lending transaction, the manual estimate was over 300 hours. Using structured AI-assisted review, the team completed the work in 100 hours — a 200% productivity increase on a deadline that manual staffing alone could not have met.19

GSK Stockmann's experience with Harvey AI on structured M&A, private equity, venture capital, and real estate diligence shows initial time savings of 15 to 20% on structured data rooms, rising to up to 75% when applied to unstructured data rooms — the kind of disorganized, poorly filed data rooms that are frequently delivered by smaller targets under seller-side time pressure. At Portolano Cavallo, the first Italian law firm to deploy Luminance, a 700-document multilingual M&A review covering Italian, English, French, and German produced a 33% time reduction compared to manual review, with the firm expecting that figure to rise with continued use of the platform.202117

Slaughter and May, using Luminance on corporate acquisitions, reported identifying critical risks 75% faster than manual methods and shortened M&A deal timelines by 30%. An AmLaw 50 firm of approximately 2,400 attorneys — without a named client in the source — reported that associates using AI-assisted review tools detected non-standard provisions at a rate 12% higher than unassisted review, because structured checklists applied systematically catch what experience and memory miss when reviewing documents sequentially.2223

These numbers vary in magnitude and in how they were measured. They are not interchangeable. But the direction of effect is consistent and the mechanism is coherent: structured extraction across a population outperforms linear sequential review both in speed and in issue coverage, because it eliminates the cross-document comparison problem that linear review cannot solve.

The Thoroughness Trade-Off: What Faster Actually Means

This is where the conversation deserves precision, because the speed argument can be misused.

Compressing diligence through better stack design is not the same as compressing diligence by accepting less coverage. The firms achieving meaningful time reductions are not skipping document categories. In most cases, they are reviewing more documents, more systematically, than they would have without the infrastructure — because the old constraint was not attorneys' willingness to be thorough, it was their capacity to be consistent across thousands of documents simultaneously.

But the risks in the redesign are real and should be named.

AI models hallucinate. This is documented and not disputed. Stanford researchers found general-purpose chatbots showed hallucination rates of 58 to 88% on legal questions. Purpose-built contract review models trained on legal corpora perform substantially better, but they are not infallible. Deloitte's Swiss practice has been explicit that AI-generated errors in M&A diligence are "neither rare nor trivial" and that a 0.5% error rate on a complex transaction can translate to millions of dollars in exposure. At 95% accuracy on a 500-page data room, a tool misclassifies or misreads approximately 25 pages of content — and those 25 pages might contain an uncapped indemnification carve-out or a consent requirement tied to the deal's most critical customer contract.2425

AI does not understand deal context. A model trained on contract language does not know that the buyer plans to consolidate operations in a way that makes certain supplier exclusivity provisions critical, or that revenue concentration in one customer makes that customer's change-of-control consent rights a potential transaction condition. Without deal-context inputs, AI treats all clauses with roughly equal weight. The issue framework described above — the pre-defined extraction targets keyed to this deal's specific risk profile — is not optional scaffolding. It is the mechanism by which deal context enters the extraction process.

Cross-reference dependencies are genuinely hard for current AI tools. A master services agreement references exhibits; those exhibits reference schedules; a side letter executed two years later amends a schedule. AI tools that analyze documents individually miss the thread. Senior attorney review of AI extraction output is not a belt-and-suspenders precaution — it is the quality control layer for a known systematic weakness.25

The precision on change-of-control clause identification is high but not absolute. AI extraction of change-of-control clauses from standard contract types runs in the high-90s precision range at AmLaw and Magic Circle firms in production deployments. The residual false-negative risk — missing a non-standard change-of-control formulation buried in an unusual contract type — is small but not zero. In a deal where two or three customer contracts represent 40% of revenue, a small miss rate applied to an 180-contract population is a manageable risk. In a deal where every individual agreement has a different bespoke structure, the miss rate warrants a different risk tolerance calibration.26

The firms navigating this most responsibly are using what has been called the "expert-in-the-loop" model: AI handles document ingestion, classification, and first-pass extraction; experienced attorneys validate findings, investigate anomalies, apply deal context, and own every judgment call that touches risk allocation or deal structure. The extraction layer eliminates the "grunt work" that partners estimated at 40% of associate time on contract review in the AmLaw 50 case study above. The attorney layer handles everything that requires reasoning about the deal rather than reading about the document.272225

Deloitte's 2025 GenAI in M&A Survey of 1,000 corporate and PE leaders found that 86% of organizations have integrated generative AI into their M&A workflows, with 65% having done so within the prior 12 months. The adoption has been rapid and recent — but data security (67%) and data quality and availability (65%) remain the leading concerns flagged by respondents. The firms scaling AI-assisted diligence are not ignoring those concerns; they are building governance frameworks around them as a precondition for adoption.282930

The California Management Review has noted that the central managerial challenge is not AI adoption itself but the redesign of M&A processes, staffing models, and governance so that deal speed does not outpace decision quality. Morgan Lewis, in a 2026 client alert, put it cleanly: AI is not a substitute for judgment, but a force multiplier for it — and when applied carelessly, it introduces risk at scale.3127

The Closing Argument

The time pressure on due diligence is structural, not cyclical, and it runs in both directions. Processes that are too short miss issues. Processes that are too long erode negotiating leverage, invite deal fatigue, and cost more than they return. The optimal zone is around 139 days for private-target transactions, per the Bayes/Intralinks analysis, and getting there without sacrificing coverage requires a stack designed for it.24

Designed, not assembled. There is a difference between deploying a contract review tool and rebuilding the review workflow around structured extraction and risk-based routing. Most firms have done the former. The latter requires deciding, before the data room opens, what you are looking for, in what priority order, with what escalation protocol when you find it — and building that logic into the process rather than relying on each associate to reconstruct it from scratch on each deal.

The speed gains from that redesign are real and documented. The risk reduction from the same redesign is also real — because consistent, population-level extraction surfaces the cross-document patterns that linear sequential review reliably misses. A thorough review is not a slow review by definition. In most cases, the thorough review is the one that was actually systematic.

The honest question for deal counsel and corporate development teams is not whether AI reads faster. It is whether the diligence workflow is designed to catch what matters — consistently, across every document in the population, in the order determined by the deal's specific risk profile rather than the data room's folder hierarchy.

If the answer is no, the slow review is already sacrificing quality. The fast review that fixes the underlying structure is the improvement.
