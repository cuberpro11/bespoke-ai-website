From CLM selection to CLM adoption: why implementations stall

An operational diagnosis for Legal Operations Directors and General Counsels who already bought a CLM system and are wondering why usage never took.

Q2 202615 MIN

The numbers are not a surprise to anyone who has lived through a CLM program. What is worth examining is how consistently they are misread.

Gartner surveyed 140 legal functions in 2024. Among CLM initiatives that had executive sponsorship, budget, and formal governance, the ones that, by conventional criteria, were "doing it right", 80% still failed to meet their defined goals. Sixty seven percent missed their original timeline. Sixty six percent exceeded their original budget. Separately, Gartner had predicted as early as 2023 that 50% of first time CLM implementations would fail to deliver expected benefits. The 2022 Onit/ContractWorks survey of 350 in house legal professionals in the US and UK found that 77% had experienced a failed technology implementation, and 43% had experienced more than one. Less than one fifth of respondents reported having never seen a problem with a legal tech rollout.12345

The standard diagnosis, insufficient sponsorship, rushed deployment, wrong vendor, is accurate as far as it goes. What it misses is that most of these programs are not failing at implementation. They are failing three to nine months after it.

The Gap Nobody Measures

Selection is a procurement exercise. It has a clear scope, a finite timeline, a budget, stakeholders who are paying attention, and a finish line labeled "contract signed." Enterprise CLM RFP cycles routinely run four to twelve months, with complex enterprise deployments taking six months or more in selection alone before a single workflow is configured. The selection phase produces deliverables: a requirements document, a vendor evaluation matrix, demo scores, a contract. People are accountable. Progress is visible.67

Implementation, by most vendors' estimates, then runs another twelve to twenty four weeks for typical deployments and up to twelve months or longer for enterprise rollouts with significant integration scope. In practice, the combined arc from "we have decided to buy a CLM" to "the system is live and users have been trained" is often twelve to eighteen months of organizational energy.87

What comes next has no name on the project plan, no budget line, and no owner. The implementation project is closed. The vendor's professional services engagement winds down or converts to support. The implementation manager who shepherded the deployment often rotates off or returns to their day job. The executive who signed the business case moves on to the next initiative. And the program is now expected to sustain and deepen adoption on its own.

It does not. The pattern is described, with some variation in framing, by every practitioner and post mortem report surveyed for this piece: adoption metrics that peak around go live, workarounds that appear within the first month, and reversion to email and spreadsheets that becomes permanent by month three. According to the 2024 Zuva survey of 80 companies, only 36% of the companies surveyed used a dedicated CLM system at all, and of those, barely more than 28% used the CLM enterprise wide rather than as one tool among several. The gap between "we bought CLM" and "CLM changed how we operate" is wide and growing.9101

The gap exists because selection and adoption are fundamentally different problems that are resourced and measured as if they were the same project. The first is a procurement judgment with defined deliverables. The second is an ongoing organizational behavior change with no finish line. Running them under a single project budget and a single project team guarantees that one of them will be stranded.

Where Programs Break

Change Management Gets One Bullet Point

Most CLM implementation plans are built around technical milestones: configuration complete, UAT passed, data migrated, go live. Change management, training, communication, adoption strategy, user feedback loops, is typically allocated a single phase near the end of the project plan and is the first item cut when the timeline slips. The result, reliably, is a technically successful implementation that no one uses.11

The Onit/ContractWorks data quantify the human cost. Beyond the failure statistics, 29% of in house counsel who experienced a failed tech rollout reported that it made them doubt whether their employer knew what was best for the business. Twenty seven percent said a failed rollout prompted them to consider leaving their position. These are not abstract ROI calculations. They describe what happens to legal ops credibility inside an organization when a program is oversold and underdelivered.4

The problem is structural. Change management for CLM is genuinely difficult because the system requires simultaneous behavior change across multiple departments, legal, procurement, sales, finance, and IT, each of which has different definitions of what "done right" looks like. Sales wants speed. Legal wants accuracy. Finance wants compliance. Procurement wants visibility. CLM forces all of these groups into a shared, standardized process, and the implementation inherits whatever conflicts exist between them. A change management plan allocated two weeks at the end of the project is not a plan for that problem; it is a deadline for the training slides.12

No One Owns the Contract Process

The second structural break is governance, or its absence. CLM implementation requires a designated process owner: someone with authority over how contracts move through the organization and accountability for making the system work after go live. In most organizations, that person does not exist before the project starts. Legal owns drafting. Procurement owns vendor agreements. Sales manages NDAs and commercial terms. No single function owns the end to end contract lifecycle, and no one has been given the authority to standardize it.11

When the authority vacuum persists into implementation, the project becomes a negotiation between competing departments rather than a coherent build. Configuration decisions get deferred. Workflows are designed by committee. Go live gets pushed, and when it finally happens, the "owner" of the system is whatever legal ops professional happened to carry the most weight in the project, usually alongside their full existing workload. The Bloomberg Law reporting on this point is direct: "It gets thrown into the ops job description," said Lucy Bassli, a consultant who works with legal departments on contracting processes. The CLM implementation will "bury that legal operations professional." She added that the task is so onerous "most of them do it once, and don't ever want to do it again".13

The executive sponsor who made the business case rarely maintains active engagement post launch. Their attention has moved to the next initiative. The legal ops professional who managed deployment transitions back to tactical work. The CLM is now live, which is read as success, and no one has a formal mandate to drive the adoption curve.

Data Migration Is Underestimated, Then Abandoned

Legacy contract data is consistently cited as the most technically underestimated part of a CLM program. Nearly all organizations hold contracts in multiple locations, SharePoint folders, shared network drives, local drives, email attachments, filing cabinets, often with inconsistent naming conventions, missing metadata, and no standardized taxonomy. According to the Zuva survey, 86% of companies use document management tools like SharePoint or Google Drive for contract storage, and a majority of them use no CLM at all, precisely because the migration complexity is prohibitive.149

When migration is attempted, it rarely goes cleanly. Metadata fields are undefined or inconsistently populated. Parent child relationships between master agreements and SOWs are mapped manually, at significant cost in time. Contracts from prior CLMs or acquisitions require translation into the new system's data model. The result is that the repository that users encounter at go live is partial, inconsistently tagged, and difficult to search, which is precisely the experience that leads users to conclude the CLM doesn't work and revert to what they know.15

The migration problem does not end at go live. It tends to either block go live (if the organization insists on migrating everything first) or sabotage adoption post launch (if users encounter a broken repository). Organizations that treat migration as a technical task rather than a governance task, deciding which contracts matter, what metadata is required, and who is responsible for data quality, consistently find themselves on the wrong side of both outcomes.

Workflows Built for the Ideal Process, Not the Actual One

Every CLM platform embeds architectural assumptions about how contracts move through an organization. Those assumptions may not match how contracts actually move in yours. When the mismatch is discovered at configuration rather than at go live, it is expensive but fixable. When it is discovered at go live, or three months post launch when lawyers start building informal workarounds, the cost is paid in adoption.11

The most common version of this failure is what practitioners describe as "automating a mess", importing the organization's existing processes into the CLM without first documenting, interrogating, or standardizing them. Undocumented edge cases that were handled by institutional knowledge in the old process become workflow failures in the new system. Lawyers, whose tolerance for friction in their contract drafting process is low by nature, find one or two friction points in the CLM and return to Word and email. The workaround, once established, is extremely difficult to reverse.16

The Bloomberg Law reporting captures the dynamic: "The only thing that happens when you put good tech with bad process is that you get more bad process," said Akshay Verma, COO of SpotDraft. When the CLM fails, the vendor typically gets the blame regardless of where the actual problem originated, a dynamic that distorts the post mortem and makes the next CLM cycle harder to justify internally.13

The Template and Clause Library Decays

Even programs that achieve solid initial adoption tend to stall once the template and clause library begins to drift. Contract language is not static. Regulatory requirements change. Market standards for indemnification, limitation of liability, and data privacy terms evolve. Business priorities shift. A clause library that was accurate at go live and has not been reviewed or updated in twelve months is a liability.

In practice, clause library maintenance is rarely owned by anyone after go live. The attorney who shepherded the initial templates has moved on to other work. No one has a calendar reminder to review the library for regulatory alignment. The legal ops professional who maintains the system typically does not have the legal authority to update substantive contract language unilaterally. The result is that the approved language in the CLM drifts out of alignment with what lawyers actually want to use. They override the templates. They pull in language from Word files. The governance function of the clause library, the reason it was built, disappears.17

Execo, a CLM operations services firm, describes this in direct operational terms: "Clause libraries and AI models lose relevance over time, and automated workflows become inefficient. As internal teams struggle to keep up, these inefficiencies increase risks and missed opportunities." This is not a hypothetical failure mode. It is the default state of an unmaintained CLM library within twelve to eighteen months of go live.17

The Post Signature Gap

There is a structural critique of the CLM category that sits underneath the operational issues: CLM platforms were built to optimize pre signature workflows, authoring, negotiation, approval, execution. That is where the category's architecture focuses, and on that problem, the mature platforms perform well.

What CLM typically does not solve, and in many cases was not designed to solve, is the post signature intelligence problem. Who is tracking obligation performance? Who monitors auto renewals? Who has visibility into what pricing mechanisms are in force across the contract portfolio? The ContractFull analysis of the Gartner 2024 data makes the point cleanly: "CLM's architecture is built around routing and automating processes, it was designed for the first half of the contract lifecycle. Making sense of what's already been signed is a structurally different problem." Many programs that technically succeed at go live still find themselves reporting the same symptoms a year later, missed renewals, invisible portfolio risk, audit prep that still hurts, because the tool they purchased addressed the authoring problem, not the portfolio intelligence problem.1

This is not a vendor indictment. It is a scoping failure. Organizations frequently enter CLM selection with a combined objective, fix the drafting process and get visibility into our contract portfolio, without distinguishing between the two problems or evaluating whether a single tool addresses both adequately. The 28% of CLM owning organizations that actually deploy enterprise wide suggests that most programs plateau well before reaching the portfolio intelligence layer.9

The Operating Pattern That Prevents It

There is no mystery about what correlates with CLM programs that sustain adoption. The evidence points in consistent directions. What is missing is less often knowledge and more often the organizational will to resource accordingly.

Process Before Platform

The programs that achieve durable adoption document and clean up the actual contract process before selecting a platform, not after. This means mapping current state workflows as they actually operate, including edge cases, exceptions, and the informal routes contracts take when the formal process is unclear, and establishing process ownership before any vendor is evaluated.1211

The Deloitte UK analysis of CLM failures identifies this sequence failure as the central one: organizations conflate CLM technology with full contract lifecycle management operations. The real business case victories, per Deloitte's assessment, come "from when an organisation simplifies its contracts and contract process, connects the legal function to the business, gets the right type of resourcing around it and then puts the technology in as an enabler." This is a workflow first, not tool first, approach, and it is structurally incompatible with the procurement driven timeline that characterizes most CLM selections, where the tool decision often precedes the process clarity.18

Dedicated, Continuous Adoption Ownership

The single most consistent predictor of CLM programs that sustain adoption is the presence of a dedicated, named owner of the CLM program who survives the go live transition and has an ongoing mandate. This is distinct from the implementation project manager, who typically exits after go live, and from the general counsel, who typically disengages after the business case is approved.19

The dedicated CLM operations role, described variously as a CLM Legal Operations specialist, a contract program manager, or a legal ops administrator, is responsible for continuous tasks that have no natural owner otherwise: managing the template and clause library, monitoring adoption metrics, soliciting user feedback, updating workflows as business processes change, coordinating with the vendor on product updates, and maintaining data quality in the repository. According to the Eric Mueller analysis, this role does not need to be a lawyer, but it does need the authority to enforce decisions across legal and operational teams, the patience to support users with varying technical proficiency, and the mandate to exist beyond the go live celebration. Absent this role, the CLM is essentially ungoverned six months after launch.19

The evidence on resourcing is consistent: legal ops functions that treat CLM as an ongoing program rather than a completed project measurably outperform those that do not. CLOC's framework for CLM adoption explicitly identifies continuous program management, with defined metrics, a named owner, and ongoing stakeholder engagement, as the foundation of sustainable adoption.201911

Embedded Workflows, Not Replacement Behaviors

Programs that achieve broad adoption tend to design CLM use so that it sits inside existing drafting habits rather than replacing them. Rather than requiring lawyers to learn a new interface for every contract type simultaneously, they identify the one or two highest volume, lowest complexity contract types and make the CLM the path of least resistance for exactly those contracts. Templates that are demonstrably better than the alternatives. Approvals that route automatically instead of through email chains. Metadata that populates without manual entry.

Ironclad's own customer analysis, admittedly vendor produced and therefore to be weighted accordingly, has consistently described this sequencing as the distinguishing characteristic of high adoption programs: start with one workflow, prove it works, then expand. The CLM literature broadly corroborates the approach without meaningful dissent. The key implication is that the phase one scope of a CLM program should be chosen not based on complexity or strategic importance but based on the likelihood of producing an experience that makes the system demonstrably easier than the alternative. Users who experience one CLM workflow as genuinely less painful than email and Word become advocates. Users whose first CLM experience is a complex negotiation workflow with half built integrations become obstacles.2112

Adoption Metrics Divorced from Go Live

Programs that sustain adoption define and track adoption metrics separately from implementation milestones. Go live is not an adoption metric; it is a technology milestone. Meaningful adoption metrics include: the percentage of in scope contracts created in the CLM rather than outside it, the template utilization rate, the average cycle time for standard contract types, and the volume of contracts in the repository with complete required metadata.

The absence of post go live adoption measurement is both a symptom and a cause. If the program is not measuring adoption, it has no mechanism to detect the quiet reversion that begins in month two or three, no visibility into which user populations are not engaging, and no data to support the business case for continued investment. Without that data, the GC cannot make the argument for the dedicated adoption owner, the ongoing template maintenance budget, or the incremental workflow expansion. The program starves.

Linking measurement back to outcomes rather than to technology milestones also reframes the executive conversation. As legal ops consultant Lucy Bassli's framing implies: the business case was never "go live by Q3." It was cycle time reduction, risk visibility, missed renewals eliminated, outside counsel spend attributable to contract quality. Measuring against those outcomes keeps the program alive. Measuring against go live ends it.13

What the Data Cannot Tell You

There are specific data points that this analysis cannot provide, and they are worth noting.

There is no rigorously controlled study with before and after data on CLM adoption rates segmented by program structure (dedicated owner vs. no dedicated owner, workflow first vs. tool first). The Gartner 2024 survey cited above provides failure rates at the program level, not behavioral data at the user level. The Onit/ContractWorks survey documents failure incidence but not the causal path from specific program decisions to specific failure modes. The practitioner literature, Deloitte, CLOC, the various CLM vendor whitepapers, describes patterns based on implementation experience rather than controlled observation.420181

The CLOC 2025 State of the Industry Report, based on data from 186 organizations, provides benchmarking on technology adoption rates and legal ops resourcing but does not isolate CLM specific adoption trajectories over time. Bloomberg Law's Legal Ops and Tech Survey (168 legal professionals, January 2024) documents that contract management tools consistently rank in the top five most used legal technologies but also that in house attorneys report "considerably higher levels of dissatisfaction with contract lifecycle management tools compared to other widely adopted legal tech tools", without drilling into the specific mechanics of why.222324

The honest characterization of the evidence base is: there is strong directional consensus from multiple independent sources about where CLM programs break and what operating patterns correlate with better outcomes. There is not yet a sufficiently rigorous longitudinal dataset to quantify those correlations at the program structure level.

The Unsentimental Takeaway

The gap between CLM selection and CLM adoption is not a technology problem, a vendor problem, or primarily a change management problem. It is a resourcing and governance problem that follows predictably from how CLM programs are structured and measured.

Selection has a project team, a budget, a timeline, and a finish line. Adoption does not. As long as that asymmetry persists, the failure rate will remain where it is, around 50% on the conservative Gartner estimate, and 80% by the more demanding metric of actually meeting defined program goals. The programs that work are not doing anything exotic. They are doing the ordinary things that adoption requires, continuous ownership, workflow first design, post go live measurement, template maintenance, and they are doing them with explicit, sustained resources rather than as afterthoughts to the implementation project.21

The broader industry trend reinforces the urgency. According to the 2025 CLOC State of the Industry Report, 62% of legal departments now have some form of CLM, up from 33% a few years earlier, while 64% of CLOs plan to increase CLM investment. The pipeline of organizations buying CLM is growing faster than the organizational capacity to adopt it. More programs beginning means, at current failure rates, more programs stalling. The question for Legal Operations Directors and General Counsels who have already made the purchase is not whether the tool works. At this point, most of the major platforms do. The question is whether the program is structured to survive go live.221

Most are not.
