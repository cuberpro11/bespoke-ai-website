Trading documentation

Standing orders as living documents

A market-structure and trading-operations analysis for trading desk heads, operations leads, and compliance officers

Q1 202616 MIN

The Gap Between the Written Mandate and the Trading Stack

A standing order — whether framed as an execution mandate, an algo parameter sheet, a smart-order-router (SOR) configuration document, or a standing settlement instruction (SSI) — is, in functional terms, a contract. One party (the desk, the client, the portfolio manager) specifies how their order flow will be handled. The other party (the execution infrastructure, the prime broker, the counterparty) agrees to act within those specifications. Legal form varies: some mandates are explicit written agreements, others exist as operational configurations embedded in an OMS or routing engine. The contractual character, however, is consistent. Breach the mandate — route orders to venues not listed, apply a participation-rate assumption that no longer matches the client's risk tolerance, settle to an account whose SWIFT details changed six months ago — and the operational, fiduciary, and regulatory consequences are real.

What distinguishes standing trading documentation from most commercial contracts is how it is subsequently managed. Commercial contracts get amended via a formal process: counterparty notification, redlining, signature. Standing orders, by contrast, are typically written once — at account opening, at strategy launch, or at the onboarding of a new algo product — and then obeyed loosely, while the market environment, the available venues, the algo's internal logic, and the client's actual risk parameters evolve around them.

The result is a structural divergence: the document says one thing; the execution stack does another. That divergence is not inherently malicious. It accumulates through ordinary operational friction — a new dark pool launches, a SOR configuration gets updated in the system but not in the written policy, an algo vendor releases a new version with altered participation caps, a client changes custodian banks without notifying the dealing desk. At any individual moment, the drift seems trivial. Over time, the written mandate becomes an archaeology of how the firm used to trade rather than a specification of how it currently trades.

FIXatdl — the FIX Trading Community's XML standard for expressing algo parameters — provides a useful illustration of the structural challenge. FIXatdl standardizes how an algorithm's controls, constraints, defaults, and validation rules are expressed across OMS and EMS platforms. It reduces bespoke integrations. But the standard describes machine-readable parameter expression. It does not, by itself, mandate that the human-readable mandate document in the compliance folder reflects what those parameters actually contain at any given moment. The machine knows. The document may not.1

What "Material Change" Actually Means in Practice

Regulatory frameworks have attempted to define the perimeter of this problem. MiFID II Article 27 requires investment firms to review their order execution policy at least annually, and whenever a material change occurs that affects the firm's ability to obtain best execution on a consistent basis. ESMA's 2025 Final Report on RTS on order execution policies goes further: it specifies that monitoring thresholds, when breached, should trigger an immediate ad hoc assessment rather than waiting for the next annual cycle. The FCA's COBS 11.2A codifies the same obligation in UK law, requiring regular monitoring of the effectiveness of execution arrangements to identify and correct deficiencies.234

For algorithmic trading specifically, MiFID II Article 17 and RTS 6 require investment firms to test and document algorithms before deployment, and to retest after any material change. ESMA's February 2026 Supervisory Briefing on Algorithmic Trading — issued in the wake of common supervisory action conducted across EU national competent authorities following the May 2022 Nordic flash crash — provides the most current regulatory articulation of what a "material change" actually encompasses. The Briefing defines a material change as any modification that may alter the behaviour, risk profile, or compliance posture of an algorithm, algorithmic trading system, or strategy. Covered change types include: alterations to execution behaviour (order types, slicing logic, routing mechanisms), changes in scope (new instruments, venues, asset classes), modifications to risk controls (thresholds, kill-switch logic, alert triggers), and — critically — any retraining or modification of machine learning components.5678

The ML point is operationally non-trivial. A gradient-boosted execution model that learns from market microstructure will, by construction, update its internal weightings continuously. When those accumulated updates shift the model's effective behaviour, the question of whether they constitute a "material change" that requires formal documentation and retesting cannot be answered by examining the written mandate. The written mandate describes the strategy intent at the time of authorization. It does not track parameter drift. ESMA specifically calls out firms' obligation to "manage the risk that a series of minor or small changes due to recalibrations could accumulate over time, when uncontrolled or unchecked, into a material change in the model output without it being tested".67

The AFM — the Dutch financial markets authority — announced in April 2026 that its supervisory priorities for the annual RTS 6 self-assessments to be requested from firms in Q3 2026 would focus precisely on this: consistent definitions of what constitutes a trading algorithm, criteria for what triggers material-change retesting, and the treatment of AI/ML risks in governance documentation. That is a regulator telling firms, in advance, where it intends to look for gaps between written governance and actual operational practice.910

Documented Consequences of Mandate Drift

Execution Quality Degradation

The most immediate operational consequence of a stale execution mandate is that the desk executes under instructions that no longer reflect current market conditions or available liquidity. A standing order that specifies routing preference for venues that have changed their maker-rebate structure, or that does not incorporate newly available dark-pool liquidity, produces systematic execution drag that is invisible to any review process that confines itself to reading the mandate document.

FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report identifies — as a persistent finding — the failure of firms to compare execution quality against competing markets, including ATSs and additional liquidity sources not currently included in routing arrangements, and the failure to modify routing logic or formally justify why routing arrangements are not being modified. The observation echoes across multiple regulatory cycles: FINRA's 2025 Report identified the same pattern under the heading of overreliance on vendors and insufficiency of regular-and-rigorous review processes. These are not edge-case findings in a handful of small broker-dealers. They reflect a structural norm: standing routing arrangements are set and then not systematically reviewed against available alternatives.111213

Transaction-cost analysis systems can detect this drift — measuring realized execution costs against benchmarks such as arrival price or VWAP — but TCA is a post-trade tool that diagnoses a condition after it has affected clients. It does not prevent the mandate from becoming stale; it records the cost of staleness after the fact.1415

Compliance Exposure: Written Policy vs. Actual Practice

The SEC's 2018 OCIE Risk Alert, drawn from deficiency letters across over 1,500 adviser examinations, identified a consistent failure mode: advisers that did not follow their own policies regarding best execution review — including seeking comparisons from competing broker-dealers — and advisers with policies that did not take into account the current business of the adviser. The phrase is precise and worth emphasis: policies that did not take into account the current business. These are advisers whose written mandates were drafted at a point in time and remained static while the actual execution behavior of the firm evolved around them.161718

The SEC's 2023 enforcement action against Manhattan-based E. Magnus Oppenheim & Co. provides a concrete example. From 2019 to 2021, the firm's written compliance policies and procedures had been adopted from another investment adviser's compliance manual — without removing references to the other firm and without tailoring the manual to its own business. The written policies did not address areas relevant to the firm's actual operations. The SEC found that EMO failed to satisfy its duty to seek best execution because it did not make an adequate assessment of commission rates, fees, or ticket charges from its clearing broker, nor did it attempt to negotiate better terms or assess alternatives. The firm had been on notice of these deficiencies since a 2019 examination — and still had not remediated them when it faced enforcement. The written policy was present. The operational substance behind it was not.19

Kaufman Rossin's guidance for fund managers synthesizes the SEC's expectations accurately: best execution review obligations apply both at the time of initial broker-dealer selection and on an ongoing basis; automated execution choices must be evaluated at least quarterly; and the evaluations must be documentable. That last requirement — documentability — is where firms consistently fail. Performing a review informally and failing to record it is, from an examiner's perspective, indistinguishable from not performing the review at all.2017

The Pre-Trade Control Calibration Problem

The May 2022 Nordic flash crash provides the clearest market-wide example of what happens when operational controls — which are themselves a form of standing instruction — are miscalibrated and not subsequently reviewed. A Citigroup trader inadvertently submitted a large sell order in European equities; inadequate pre-trade controls failed to flag the error before execution. The resulting cascade wiped approximately €300 billion from European equity markets within minutes before a partial recovery. UK regulators subsequently fined Citigroup £62 million. The key operational failure was not that pre-trade controls were absent. It was that the controls in place were not calibrated to catch the specific type of error that occurred — and that the governance process for reviewing and updating those controls had not kept pace with the firm's trading activity.212223

ESMA's 2026 Supervisory Briefing was issued directly in response to this event and the CSA that followed. Its conclusion is clear: investment firms must implement "hard blocks" — strict default limits on price, value, and volume that cannot be overridden by a trader — but they must also periodically review and recalibrate those blocks to ensure they remain appropriate. A control calibrated at account opening for a desk that trades €5 million per day does not remain adequate when the same desk begins trading €500 million per day. The standing instruction needs to be a living configuration, not a point-in-time setting.724

Settlement Failure from Stale SSIs

Standing settlement instructions occupy the operational endpoint of the same problem. The FMSB's January 2025 Standard for Sharing of SSIs states explicitly that the most significant cause of settlement fails — after lack of inventory — is incorrect or missing SSIs. The Standard describes the structural drivers: non-standard format, infrequent SSI exchange cadences, and a high percentage of manual exchanges that depend on the recipient to ingest, verify, and update internal databases.2526

Core Principle 7 of the FMSB Standard requires that SSIs be reviewed when they have not been used in the past 12 months or when a trade using them has failed settlement — and be amended or deleted as appropriate. Core Principle 9 requires that where a firm cannot give effect to the earlier principles, it must review and remediate obstacles no less frequently than annually. These are best-practice standards, not binding regulation, but they encode the same underlying logic: a standing instruction that is not regularly validated against current operational reality is a latent settlement risk.25

ISDA published its own Standing Settlement Instructions Suggested Operational Practices in September 2024, updating a document last issued in 2010. The update itself is evidence of the governance gap: it took fourteen years for the industry body to reissue best-practice guidance on a document type that underpins settlement for OTC derivatives.27

The FCA's Algorithmic Controls Review

The FCA's multi-firm review of algorithmic trading controls — conducted under the RTS 6 framework and published July 2025 — found specific and recurring evidence of documentation staleness. In reviewing firms' self-assessments and validation reports, the FCA identified:28

  • Out-of-date policies and unclear processes and documentation indicating a lack of formal governance and accountability28
  • Key policy documentation not linked or referenced in self-assessments28
  • Firms with inadequate definitions of what constitutes a material change, with no management information provided to the board regarding deployments28
  • Firms using third-party algorithms that did not have adequate technical understanding of how those algorithms were developed28
  • Pre-trade and post-trade controls with poorly defined ownership, not reflected in policies28

These findings span firms of different sizes. The FCA notes that larger firms generally had more comprehensive approaches, but that medium and small firms — even taking proportionality into account — had significant gaps between their documented controls and their operational reality.28

The Central Bank of Ireland's earlier "Dear CEO" letter on algorithmic trading firms drew similar conclusions following its own thematic review: documentation and testing procedures remained the most common area of deficiency, with firms treating their written governance materials as static artifacts rather than operationally maintained specifications.29

Treating the Mandate as a Living Document: What That Actually Requires

Reframing standing orders as living, continuously governed documents is not a technology purchase. It is an operational discipline that requires four specific capabilities: systematic reconciliation, version control, event-triggered review, and auditable decision logging. Each has concrete operational mechanics.

Systematic Reconciliation Between Written Mandate and Actual Execution Behavior

The written execution mandate and the actual execution behavior of the desk will always diverge unless someone is systematically comparing them. That comparison requires two things: a machine-readable representation of the written mandate, and a post-trade record of actual execution behavior at sufficient granularity to detect deviations.

The comparison is not a TCA exercise in the conventional sense. TCA measures execution quality against market benchmarks (VWAP, arrival price, implementation shortfall). Mandate reconciliation measures execution behavior against the written specification itself: Were orders routed only to venues listed in the mandate? Was the participation rate within the specified range? Was the algo family applied consistent with the strategy parameters? These are structural questions about conformance, not performance questions about quality. They can, in principle, be automated — but only if the mandate itself is expressed in a structured, machine-parseable format rather than as a narrative prose document.

FIX Orchestra — a FIX Trading Community initiative to migrate normative FIX protocol documentation to an XML representation — represents movement in this direction at the protocol layer. The FIX Community's active workstream on "Algo Trading Execution Data – Gap analysis," seeking more granular algo execution data fields covering (i) algo order instructions, (ii) child order routing information, and (iii) order status updates, reflects industry recognition that the granularity needed to close the reconciliation loop is currently missing from the standard data capture.30

At the operations level, reconciliation requires someone with ownership — a named function, not a generalized compliance obligation — to run the comparison on a defined cadence and produce evidence that it occurred.

Version Control for Trading Documentation

The concept of version control is well-established in software engineering. Every change to a codebase is tagged with a timestamp, an author, a description of the change, and a reference to the prior state. Rollback is straightforward. Audit is trivial.

Trading documentation receives almost none of this treatment. A standing order or execution mandate typically exists as a Word document or PDF in a compliance folder. When it is updated — if it is updated — the prior version may or may not be preserved. The change may or may not be recorded. The person who made the change may or may not be identifiable. The date of the change is rarely formally tracked.

Under RTS 6, investment firms engaged in algorithmic trading are required to timestamp, approve, and record all material changes to algorithms. The ESMA 2026 Supervisory Briefing states that testing documentation must be comprehensive and updated. The FIA's Algorithmic Trading Due Diligence Guide requires firms to confirm they retain a record of any material change to the algorithmic trading system. These are all requirements for a version-controlled record of the operational specification. Most firms meet this requirement at the code level (because software development tools enforce it automatically) but not at the written mandate level (because document management tools typically do not).316

Effective version control for trading documentation requires, at minimum: a defined document identifier for each standing order, a formal change procedure (not informal email updates), a record of what changed, who authorized the change, and when, and a defined distribution process so that all functions holding copies of the document receive the updated version simultaneously.

Event-Triggered Review Cadences

Annual review, as required under MiFID II Article 27 and RTS 6 Article 9, is a floor, not a target. The regulatory text specifies that material changes — venue closures, algo version updates, significant changes in market structure, threshold breaches identified during monitoring — require an immediate, ad hoc review rather than waiting for the annual cycle.42

This means effective mandate governance requires not just a periodic review calendar but an event registry: a defined list of trigger events that automatically initiate a review of the affected standing orders. Practical triggers include:

  • An execution venue entering or exiting the firm's approved list
  • A vendor releasing a new version of an algo that changes default parameters or routing logic
  • A monitoring threshold breach detected in the regular-and-rigorous review process
  • A regulatory change affecting venue obligations (e.g., trading obligation shifts under MiFIR)
  • A counterparty notifying a change to settlement account details
  • A strategy deploying to a new instrument or asset class for the first time
  • An ML model completing a retraining cycle that may have altered its effective behavior profile

The trigger list is not closed. But the key operational requirement is that it exists formally and that responsibility for evaluating whether a trigger event has occurred is assigned to a specific function — not left to whoever happens to notice.

The ISS Best Execution Compliance Process framework describes the ongoing monitoring obligation as requiring systematic reporting and review of trading costs for outliers — orders with costs outside the range of normal trading costs. That is a monitoring mechanism, not a reconciliation mechanism. The two are complementary: monitoring detects deviation from expected execution performance; reconciliation detects deviation from written mandate specification.32

Auditable Decision Logging

The SEC's OCIE observations and FINRA's recurring best-execution findings share a common structural failure: reviews that may have occurred but were not documented, decisions that may have been reasonable but cannot be demonstrated as such to an examiner. The evidentiary standard is not merely that a firm made a sound judgment; it is that the firm can prove it made the judgment, by reference to contemporaneous documentation.1316

For standing-order governance specifically, this requires a decision log that records: the date of each review, what was reviewed, what the review concluded, whether any change was made to the mandate, the basis for that conclusion, and who authorized it. Where the review concluded no change was needed, the log should record the specific findings that supported that conclusion — not a generic "policy reviewed, no changes required" entry. The latter provides no evidentiary value. The former demonstrates that a substantive review actually occurred.

The Kaufman Rossin framework for fund managers describes this precisely: "Develop a framework for documenting the steps you've taken to determine best execution. This may be as simple as a checklist or a form that you fill out each time you make a decision or evaluate automated execution systems". The mechanism does not need to be elaborate. It needs to be consistent, timestamped, and retained.20

Under Regulation NMS Rule 606, broker-dealers are already required to publish quarterly reports on order routing practices. FINRA Rule 6151 requires members to submit those reports to FINRA for centralized publication. These disclosures are generated from actual routing data. The compliance gap arises when the written execution policy does not reflect the routing practices being disclosed — a direct and demonstrable mismatch between the document and the operation.3313

Ownership and Accountability

The structural reason standing orders accumulate drift is that no single function owns both the document and the execution behavior simultaneously. The portfolio manager owns the investment mandate. The trading desk owns the routing logic. Operations owns the SSIs. Compliance owns the written policy. Technology owns the OMS configuration. None of these functions has a defined obligation to compare their own output against the outputs of the adjacent functions.

Closing that gap requires explicit ownership assignment: a named role (or function) whose responsibility is to maintain the correspondence between the written mandate and the operational configuration. This role must have read access to both the document repository and the execution data. It must have a defined review cadence and a defined escalation path for discrepancies. It must produce documented evidence of its reviews.

This is, in essence, the logic the FCA's multi-firm review articulates when it identifies "out of date policies and unclear processes and documentation indicating a lack of formal governance and accountability" as a failure mode. The failure is not absent intent. It is absent ownership.28

The Architecture in Brief

The five capabilities above operate as four concurrent processes, each with a specific owner, cadence, and evidence requirement:

1. Baseline maintenance — every standing order has a unique identifier, a version number, a designated owner, and a structured representation sufficient for machine comparison against execution data. Changes follow a formal procedure.

2. Continuous monitoring — execution data is systematically compared against mandate specifications on a defined cadence (daily for high-volume strategies, weekly or monthly for lower-frequency mandates). Deviations above defined thresholds generate formal alerts.

3. Event-triggered review — a defined list of trigger events initiates an immediate mandate review, outside the periodic cadence. Reviews are logged with finding and disposition.

4. Periodic formal review — at minimum annually, all standing orders are subject to a comprehensive review against current market conditions, available venues, regulatory requirements, and strategy parameters. Reviews produce documented findings, not just approval signatures.

These processes do not require bespoke technology. They require process design, ownership assignment, and documentation discipline — the same operational infrastructure that any well-run contract management function applies to commercial agreements.

Closing Line

A standing order that is not continuously reconciled against actual execution behavior is not a mandate. It is a historical artifact that creates the appearance of governance while providing none of the substance.
