Repapering at scale: what ISDA migrations teach us about AI readiness

An institutional analysis for General Counsels, COOs, and Heads of Legal Operations in capital markets

Q2 202615 MIN

The LIBOR transition was, by the ARRC's own account in its 2023 closing report, "one of the most complicated, if not the most complicated, changes in financial market infrastructure ever required." It consumed billions of dollars and millions of hours of private-sector labor, required an act of Congress for the most intractable legacy contracts, and still produced, in the months before the June 30, 2023 cessation of USD LIBOR, a live warning from the ARRC of a potential "operational Armageddon." The benchmark transition was, in other words, a controlled stress test for the entire legal operations infrastructure of the capital markets — and a number of institutions visibly failed it on the first attempt. What separated those that managed the exercise from those that spent most of it in triage was not, primarily, the sophistication of their negotiating strategy or the size of their outside counsel budget. It was whether they could answer a single foundational question before the clock started: what is actually in our back book?123

The scale of the problem

Begin with the numbers, because the sheer magnitude is necessary context. At peak, LIBOR was embedded in an estimated $400 trillion of financial instruments globally — derivatives, bonds, loans, consumer products, and a range of nonfinancial contracts. The ARRC estimated that USD LIBOR alone underpinned roughly $200 trillion of financial contracts — approximately ten times U.S. GDP. As of the final year of the transition, the ARRC identified approximately $74 trillion in USD LIBOR-referencing derivatives outstanding beyond the June 2023 cessation date, alongside roughly $5 trillion in legacy cash products (loans and securities) that would need active remediation.45231

These aggregate numbers, however, understate the operational problem. Notional exposures can be hedged, netted, or flowed through protocols. What cannot be shortcut is document-by-document assessment: determining, for each counterparty relationship, whether an agreement has a workable fallback, whether it falls within the scope of the ISDA 2020 IBOR Fallbacks Protocol, and — if bilateral remediation is required — what specific amendment language applies. For derivative contracts alone, that meant reviewing the legacy 2006 ISDA Definitions, confirming existing CSA and Schedule terms, and in many cases going back to individual paper confirmations executed over decades.

The ISDA 2020 IBOR Fallbacks Protocol, launched in October 2020 and effective January 25, 2021, ultimately attracted over 16,000 adhering parties. At launch, 257 firms had already adhered during the pre-launch escrow period. By mid-2021, early surveys found nearly 50% of firms had signed or planned to sign the protocol, though 23% had no plans to do so and 28% were uncertain. Even for protocol adherents, adherence addressed only the derivatives documented under ISDA's standard framework. It did not cover agreements with bespoke fallback language, it did not address structured notes or loans, and it emphatically did not eliminate the need to understand what a firm's actual legacy exposure looked like before making an adherence decision.678

The Uncleared Margin Rules (UMR) added a parallel documentation crisis. Phases 4 and 5 of UMR — capturing firms with AANA thresholds down to €8 billion — brought approximately 1,200 new firms into scope for initial margin, between them managing roughly 9,500 counterparty relationships. Each of those relationships required negotiation and execution of new Credit Support Annexes, custodial agreements, and in many cases full ISDA documentation for previously undocumented counterparty pairs. The documentation volume was not at the same scale as LIBOR, but the timeline pressure was comparable: firms entering scope for the first time had to simultaneously discover which relationships existed, assess current documentation status, and negotiate new CSA terms — all under regulatory deadlines with real enforcement consequences.9

Why extraction is the bottleneck

The standard account of why repapering cycles are hard focuses on the legal complexity: negotiation with counterparties, drafting bespoke amendments, managing bilateral consent. That account is accurate but incomplete. The more fundamental bottleneck, consistently underappreciated until firms are already in the middle of a repapering wave, is data extraction from the existing back book.

The problem has a structural cause. Derivatives agreements — ISDA Master Agreements, Schedules, CSAs, and the confirmations that give them operative meaning — accumulated over decades in formats that were never intended to be queried at scale. A large dealer's legacy book might comprise Master Agreements executed in the 1990s under the 1992 ISDA, re-documented under the 2002 ISDA at various points, layered with amendments, side letters, and protocol adherence records, supplemented by confirmations in multiple formats, and stored as scanned PDFs or physical files with variable-quality OCR metadata. The iManage RAVN team noted, based on their work with financial institutions during this period, that approximately 80% of information in institutional document repositories is unstructured and carries no useful metadata — making it effectively unsearchable for LIBOR-relevant provisions without active extraction.10

The practical consequence is that "knowing your book" is not a trivial lookup. A firm that wants to know which of its outstanding OTC derivatives reference LIBOR after 2021, which have workable fallbacks, and which require bilateral renegotiation must first extract the governing terms from each document. For a major dealer with a portfolio of tens of thousands of counterparty relationships — each potentially spanning multiple agreements, amendments, and confirmations — manual review at conventional paralegal rates is not economically feasible within the available timeline. Even conservatively, the cost of doing this work manually at law firm billing rates runs to millions of pounds or dollars before a single amendment has been negotiated.10

The Bloomberg/PRMIA survey conducted in early 2022 across 130 executives at firms globally found that 82% of respondents identified "repapering existing contracts and agreements" as a challenge — the same percentage that identified systems and operational readiness as a hurdle. By the follow-up Bloomberg survey in early 2023, the percentage citing repapering as a challenge had dropped to 36%, suggesting that most firms had cleared the extraction bottleneck by then — but only after two additional years of effort and under the looming pressure of a hard cessation date.1112

The EY Third Annual IBOR Transition Readiness Survey in 2021 found that technology and legal repapering together constituted over half of total IBOR transition program spend across banks. The majority of G-SIBs were expected to spend more than $100 million each in 2021 on transition-related costs, with most expecting spending to remain above $50 million into 2022 and 2023. The Accenture 2019 survey of 127 financial services institutions — conducted before the crunch — found that only 15% of respondents said their legal teams were ready to handle the volume of contract remediation, deal restructuring, and repapering activities ahead of them, and only 18% rated their overall transition programs as "mature." These are not indicators of firms with their back books organized.131415

The scanned-document problem deserves specific attention, because it is less discussed than the sheer volume challenge. Legacy ISDA agreements from the 1990s and early 2000s frequently exist only as scanned images, not as text-searchable PDFs. OCR quality on these documents varies dramatically: proper nouns in party names, rate definitions, and IBOR-specific provisions that appear in schedule elections are exactly the kinds of terms that OCR renders inconsistently. A search for "LIBOR" across a document repository might return 70% of the relevant documents if you are fortunate, missing the remainder because of scanning artifacts, non-standard formatting, or provisions cross-referenced to standalone annexes that are filed separately. Academic work on extracting structured data from scanned contracts has long noted the particular difficulty of hierarchically structured data and tables — precisely the format used for CSA elections. Any firm that assumed its document management system gave it a reliable count of LIBOR-referencing agreements before commencing a review was likely working with a materially incomplete picture.16

The IHS Markit Outreach360 product, marketed specifically for LIBOR repapering, described the workflow precisely: the first phase was "Discovery" — organizing tens of thousands of contracts by impact (fallback language, amendment provisions) — before any outreach or negotiation could begin. The sequencing is telling. You cannot negotiate amendments until you know what the existing terms are. You cannot know the existing terms without extracting them. And you cannot extract them from legacy PDFs without either substantial manual labor or purpose-built technology. For firms that had not previously invested in structured contract data, the discovery phase became the repapering cycle.17

What "finishing ahead" actually looked like

A number of firms demonstrably cleared the extraction problem before the crunch, and the operational consequences were material. The pattern is visible, even where the specifics of individual firm programs are not publicly disclosed.

Goldman Sachs had invested in document AI infrastructure for regulatory compliance before the LIBOR transition began, specifically building out Eigen Technologies' NLP platform for Dodd-Frank Qualified Financial Contract (QFC) recordkeeping requirements. The QFC program required Goldman to extract 49 data points from more than 50 different document types at a rate of up to 1,500 documents per day. When the LIBOR transition required document analysis, Goldman was able to pivot the same infrastructure: as reported in Eigen's published case study, the platform "accelerating the project timescales drastically" for LIBOR document analysis, and Goldman's team noted that "the flexible nature of the platform would enable us to use it for other document and data needs, including BRRD adherence and LIBOR transition." The firm was not building document analysis capability from scratch when LIBOR transition arrived; it was extending a working system.18

ING's experience provides the clearest public window into what the operational advantage looks like in quantitative terms. ING deployed Eigen's NLP technology for its IBOR Transition Program within its Wholesale Banking division, applying it to automated review of contracts referencing IBOR benchmarks. Eigen reported that this delivered time savings of 75% and cost savings of 60% relative to the manual baseline. ING and Eigen won the Financial Times Intelligent Business Award for Financial Services in 2020 for this program. ING's partnership with Eigen was formalized and expanded in March 2020 — before the ISDA Fallbacks Protocol was even launched — meaning that ING had operational NLP infrastructure in place for LIBOR work at the precise moment the industry was beginning to realize the scale of the document problem.1920

BlackRock was also a noted Eigen client during this period, as was Allen & Overy on the legal services side. Eigen reported that 40% of all G-SIBs used its platform to address document and data challenges, with use cases spanning LIBOR, QFC recordkeeping, EMIR, Dodd-Frank, and GDPR compliance. The common thread across these institutions is not that they adopted AI technology specifically for LIBOR — it is that they had built structured data extraction capability for prior regulatory compliance exercises, and LIBOR repapering was the first event at which having that infrastructure versus not having it became directly and publicly visible in program execution speed.2122

The firms that had not built this capability faced a different experience. As Accenture documented in its 2019 survey, a significant proportion of firms lacked "a unified and consistent approach across business lines" for transition planning, and fewer than one in five considered themselves operationally ready — defined specifically as having "operationalized systems, remediated legal agreements, fully documented product flows, and compliance to regulatory requirements." Those firms that lacked mature programs faced a particularly unappealing decision tree in 2021 and 2022: hire incremental legal resource to perform manual review at scale, engage managed-service alternatives, or fall back on the ISDA protocol for derivatives while hoping that the back book's bespoke provisions were covered. Several did all three simultaneously, at significant cost, under time pressure.13

The EY survey found that the shortage of skilled legal resources was "a clear risk for banks" and that among those banks that did not have sufficient in-house resource to transition legacy contracts, the top responses were to hire additional roles or tender out for further legal support. This is the expensive alternative to having a working extraction system: competing in a seller's market for legal resource during the same window when every major institution in the market is doing the same thing.15

The ARRC's warning about "operational Armageddon" in its July 2022 LIBOR Legacy Playbook — issued with a full year still remaining before the June 2023 USD LIBOR cessation — was specifically addressed at the institutions that still had not completed assessments of their legacy book's fallback language. The ARRC noted that "many law departments, including those of small and mid-sized banks, lack sufficient staff to perform this type of analysis." This language, from a body that had been working on LIBOR transition since 2014, reflects the accumulated frustration of watching firms defer the discovery problem until the final year of a decade-long transition.31

The argument about AI readiness

It is worth being precise about the argument this evidence supports, because the AI discourse in capital markets legal operations has become cluttered with a parallel argument that is both more exciting and less useful.

The more exciting argument — the one that dominates conference programs and vendor materials — is that generative AI will transform derivatives documentation by drafting amendments, summarizing complex agreements, and flagging anomalous provisions in near-real-time. That argument is not wrong in the long run, and some of those capabilities are materializing. But it addresses the back half of a repapering exercise: the drafting, negotiation, and amendment workflow that follows extraction. The LIBOR experience suggests the back half is not where programs fail. Programs fail in the discovery and triage phase, before counterparty outreach has begun, when legal operations teams are trying to answer the foundational question of scope.

The firms that were structurally advantaged during the LIBOR repapering had solved a different and more mundane problem: they had treated their existing contract portfolio as a queryable dataset. This is not a generative AI problem. It is a structured data extraction problem — converting unstructured text in legacy PDFs, scanned agreements, and document management systems into structured records that can be sorted, filtered, and prioritized. ING's 75% time saving was achieved with NLP-based extraction technology, not a large language model generating draft amendments. Goldman's ability to pivot its QFC infrastructure to LIBOR analysis rested on having already built a working extraction pipeline for a prior compliance requirement.2018

The distinction matters because the investment and organizational posture required are different. Building generative AI capacity for document drafting is, in some respects, easier — it relies on commercially available models, requires relatively limited proprietary training data, and can be grafted onto existing workflows incrementally. Building a reliable structured inventory of an existing derivatives back book is harder: it requires document digitization, extraction model training on institution-specific formats, data quality validation, and integration with collateral management and trade documentation systems. It is unglamorous infrastructure work. It is also, as the LIBOR cycle demonstrated, the work that determines whether a firm spends a regulatory repapering cycle in control of its timeline or chasing it.

The Accenture finding that firms with "mature" transition programs — those with operationalized systems and remediated legal agreements — were far more likely to see LIBOR transition as a strategic opportunity rather than a compliance burden is consistent with this framing. The firms that had done the infrastructure work were not just faster at repapering; they were positioned to understand their book well enough to make commercial decisions during the transition, not just compliance decisions.13

What the next cycle looks like

The LIBOR transition did not close the book on mandatory repapering. Several waves are in progress or approaching.

The ISDA 2025 – 2002 Equity Derivatives Definitions (Versionable Edition) Protocol, opened for general adherence in October 2025 with an industry go-live date of October 26, 2026, requires equity derivatives market participants to update their master confirmation agreements to reference the new definitions framework. As of mid-2026, only 48 parties had formally adhered to the protocol. The transition is designed to be less disruptive than LIBOR — the Versionable Edition is largely substantively identical to the 2002 Equity Definitions at launch, with the primary benefit being that future updates can be incorporated without bilateral negotiation. But participants that have not catalogued their existing equity derivatives master confirmation agreements still need to identify what is in scope before they can make an adherence decision or plan bilateral amendments.23242526

The 2026 FX Definitions, jointly published by ISDA and EMTA in March 2026, replace the 1998 FX and Currency Option Definitions as market standard for FX derivatives transactions, with a mandatory implementation date of November 22, 2027. From that date, Swift is no longer expected to support the 1998 definitions. The new definitions consolidate the 1998 framework, subsequent supplements, and EMTA templates into a single integrated document and eliminate the need for separate master confirmation agreements — a genuinely significant structural change for FX desks that have accumulated bilateral MCAs over nearly three decades. ISDA's own assessment is that the transition will require "resources for some institutions" to update systems, templates, and controls. Notably, unlike the LIBOR fallback protocol, ISDA is not planning to issue a protocol for updating legacy FX transactions — bilateral updating will be required for existing MCAs.27282930

The ongoing phase-in of initial margin requirements under the Uncleared Margin Rules continues to draw smaller buy-side firms into scope, each requiring documentation of new custodial arrangements and CSA terms. The ISDA Margin Survey for year-end 2025 reported $1.6 trillion of initial and variation margin collected by leading market participants, a 9.3% increase from the prior year. As the institutional scope of margin rules continues to expand, the documentation cycle continues.31

Beyond specific regulatory events, the ARRC's closing report — its final institutional message after a decade of work — elevated appropriate fallback language and active contract review to permanent best-practice obligations, not transition-specific tasks. The message is that the conditions that created the LIBOR documentation problem — contracts written without robust fallbacks, back books that were never treated as structured data assets, regulatory change arriving faster than legal infrastructure could adapt — are inherent features of the derivatives documentation landscape, not anomalies specific to LIBOR.2

The structural lesson

Firms that built extraction infrastructure before LIBOR arrived — for QFC recordkeeping, for EMIR reporting, for any prior exercise that required systematic interrogation of their document estate — spent the LIBOR cycle in a fundamentally different operational position than firms that treated their contract portfolio as a filing system. The former could prioritize their back book by exposure and fallback type within weeks of the transition timeline becoming clear. The latter spent those same weeks discovering, at considerable expense, what was in their files.

The 2026 FX Definitions transition gives firms with large FX derivatives books roughly 16 months from publication to the November 2027 implementation date to inventory their existing MCAs and plan their remediation approach. The equity derivatives protocol go-live is already on the clock. Both represent opportunities to apply the LIBOR lesson before the next cycle peaks. Firms that wait for a formal regulatory directive or a hard cessation date before asking what is in their FX derivatives documentation estate are following the same script that produced the ARRC's "operational Armageddon" language in 2022.

The central argument of the LIBOR cycle, stated plainly, is this: AI readiness in capital markets legal operations is not primarily a question of whether you have deployed a generative AI drafting tool. It is a question of whether your existing contract portfolio is a queryable dataset — whether you can answer, today, without starting a manual review project, which of your agreements reference a specific rate or provision, which have workable fallbacks, and which require bilateral attention. The firms that could answer that question before LIBOR transition began spent the cycle as counterparties worth doing business with. The firms that couldn't spent it as counterparties worth calling repeatedly.

The next protocol deadline is already in the calendar. Whether your back book is ready for it is a question only your document infrastructure can answer.
