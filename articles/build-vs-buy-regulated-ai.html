AI governance

The build-vs-buy question for regulated AI

An advisory memo for CTOs, Chief Data Officers, and General Counsels at regulated financial institutions and law firms

Q4 202521 MIN

Why This Is a Governance Question, Not a Procurement Question

The default framing for AI vendor decisions in most enterprises is procurement: weigh the cost of licensing a third-party tool against the cost of building internal capability, factor in time-to-market, and choose accordingly. In unregulated industries, that framing is mostly adequate. In regulated financial services and legal practice, it is dangerously incomplete.

When a bank deploys a credit-scoring model, a fraud-detection system, or a generative AI tool that touches customer communications, the institution is not just procuring software — it is creating regulatory exposure that attaches to the output, the data processed, and the decision pathway, regardless of whether a vendor built the underlying model. The same logic applies to law firms: placing confidential client information into an AI system is not a software purchasing decision, it is a professional conduct decision governed by duties that no vendor contract can transfer or disclaim.

The U.S. Treasury's December 2024 report on AI in financial services identified third-party AI providers as one of the sector's primary risk vectors, recommending that firms prioritize review of AI use cases for compliance before deployment and reevaluate periodically thereafter. That review cannot be delegated to the vendor. Its substance — what data the system touches, what decisions it informs, how its outputs can be audited and challenged — is defined by your regulatory obligations, not by the vendor's terms of service.1

The build-vs-buy question, properly understood, is therefore a governance question first: Who is accountable for this system's behavior, what regulatory obligations flow from that accountability, and which deployment architecture is consistent with discharging those obligations? Cost and speed remain relevant inputs — but they are second-order factors that operate within the constraints established by the first-order governance analysis.

The Regulatory Landscape: What Institutions Are Actually Navigating

Before applying a decision framework, it is worth being precise about the regulatory obligations that make the governance question hard. The landscape is fragmented, and institutions rarely face just one regime.

Banking: Model Risk Management in Transition

For over a decade, SR 11-7 — the 2011 interagency supervisory guidance on model risk management issued by the Federal Reserve and OCC — served as the de facto governance template for any quantitative system a bank deployed. Its core requirements — conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, outcomes analysis, independent validation, and effective challenge — became standard components of model inventories across large institutions. The FDIC adopted the guidance in 2017, making it effectively universal for federally supervised banks.234

In April 2026, the OCC, Federal Reserve, and FDIC jointly superseded SR 11-7 with revised interagency guidance (SR 26-2 / OCC Bulletin 2026-13). The revision is significant on several dimensions. The new guidance narrows the definition of "model," explicitly excluding generative and agentic AI from its formal scope on the grounds that these technologies are "novel and rapidly evolving." It raises the primary applicability threshold to $30 billion in assets. And it adopts a more principles-based approach, removing the mandatory annual validation cadence and replacing prescriptive board responsibilities with a general observation that model risk management "benefits from clear roles and responsibilities with well-defined accountability."54

What the revision does not do is exempt generative AI from governance. The agencies explicitly stated that GenAI tools "are not exempted from risk management and governance expectations, but rather, are subject to the risk management and governance expectations that apply to banking organizations in general." They also announced a forthcoming request for information that will address AI governance directly, including generative and agentic AI. For institutions currently operating in the gap between the old guidance and the anticipated dedicated AI framework, the practical implication is clear: the foundational principles of SR 11-7 — sound governance, independent validation, effective challenge, vendor oversight — remain the operative reference, even if they must now be applied through each institution's broader risk governance framework rather than the formal model risk program.45

In parallel, the U.S. Treasury released in February 2026 the Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework (FS AI RMF), a voluntary, industry-led adaptation of the NIST AI RMF that includes 230 mapped control objectives translating NIST principles into operational controls. The FS AI RMF is structured around four functions — Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage — and is explicitly designed to sit on top of existing model risk programs. It is not a regulatory requirement. It is, however, rapidly becoming the vocabulary in which regulators and examiners will discuss AI governance, making alignment strategically sensible even if it is not mandatory.678

Banking: Third-Party and Vendor Risk

The 2023 interagency guidance on third-party risk management — issued jointly by the OCC, Federal Reserve, and FDIC — establishes the overarching framework for how banks must manage AI vendor relationships. The guidance applies to all banks with third-party relationships and covers the full risk management lifecycle: due diligence before contracting, ongoing monitoring during the relationship, and contingency planning for vendor disruption.9

For AI specifically, the practical implications are substantial. When a bank routes customer data or proprietary model inputs through a third-party AI system — whether a large language model API, a vendor-hosted credit underwriting tool, or a compliance monitoring platform — the bank remains responsible for understanding what the system does with that data, how outputs are generated, and whether those outputs can be audited in response to a supervisory examination. As the OCC's Acting Comptroller noted in a June 2024 speech, concentrated reliance on a small number of foundation model providers creates correlated risks that individual firm-level controls cannot fully contain.10

The GLBA Safeguards Rule, updated by the FTC in June 2023, requires financial institutions to maintain comprehensive security programs covering AI systems that access nonpublic personal information (NPI), to oversee AI vendors as service providers, and to implement access controls, encryption, and incident response procedures. When a vendor's AI system processes NPI, the vendor becomes a regulated service provider under GLBA — but the regulated institution retains accountability for the vendor's conduct.11

Banking: Capital Markets Regulatory Context

FINRA has maintained a technologically neutral posture: existing rules apply to AI just as they apply to any other technology. Its 2025 Regulatory Oversight Report observed that firms are "proceeding cautiously with their use of Gen AI technology, generally exploring or implementing vendor-supported Gen AI tools to increase efficiency of internal functions." The 2026 report went further, noting that GenAI implicates obligations around supervision, communications, recordkeeping, and fair dealing, and flagging autonomous AI agents as presenting "novel regulatory and supervisory considerations."121314

On recordkeeping, SEC Rule 17a-4 requires broker-dealers to maintain electronic records in non-rewriteable, non-erasable format — a requirement that extends to AI-generated content that constitutes a business communication or record. FINRA Rule 4511 creates parallel obligations. When a GenAI tool produces output that a firm uses in a customer-facing communication, client recommendation, or supervisory record, that output and its context may be subject to retention requirements regardless of whether a vendor generated it.151617

The SEC's proposed rule on predictive data analytics conflicts — which would have created affirmative obligations for broker-dealers and investment advisers using AI in client-facing recommendations — was formally withdrawn in June 2025, signaling that the current administration does not intend to finalize that particular rulemaking. That withdrawal does not, however, alter the existing framework: investment advisers have fiduciary duties requiring conflicts management, and broker-dealers remain subject to Regulation Best Interest. AI tools that generate client recommendations must be evaluated and supervised under those standards whether or not a specific AI rule exists.1819

European Union: AI Act Obligations for Financial Services

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) entered into force in August 2024 and applies in phases. For financial institutions with EU operations, the operative date for the bulk of high-risk AI obligations under Annex III — including documentation, human oversight, data governance, and logging requirements — is August 2, 2026, absent formal adoption of the proposed Digital Omnibus delay. As of this writing, that delay has not been formally enacted, and institutions planning around December 2027 are taking a legislative gamble.20

Under Annex III, two financial sector use cases are classified as high-risk: AI used to evaluate creditworthiness or establish credit scores for natural persons, and AI used for risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance. High-risk classification triggers the full complement of AI Act obligations: data governance documentation, technical documentation, logging of system operation, transparency to deployers and affected persons, human oversight mechanisms, and conformity assessment.2120

For financial institutions that are deployers (rather than providers) of AI systems, the EU AI Act creates important upstream dependencies. Since August 2025, providers of general-purpose AI (GPAI) models have been required to make available technical documentation and downstream information under Article 53 — documentation that deployer institutions should be actively demanding from vendors for their compliance files. When a bank or insurer wraps a GPAI model via API in a credit-scoring or insurance pricing context, it assumes the role of provider of the specific AI system, triggering the full set of high-risk obligations.20

The EBA's November 2025 AI Act mapping exercise found no fundamental contradictions between the AI Act and existing EU banking legislation, but identified areas requiring integration effort — particularly around data governance, logging, and the co-existence of multiple supervisory authorities.21

Law Firms: Professional Conduct Obligations

The regulatory landscape for law firms is structurally different from banking but operationally equally constraining. The ABA issued Formal Opinion 512 in July 2024 — the first ABA ethics guidance specifically addressing generative AI — applying existing Model Rules to AI use without creating a separate AI-specific framework. The opinion identifies confidentiality (Model Rule 1.6), competence (Model Rule 1.1), communication (Model Rule 1.4), and fees (Model Rule 1.5) as the primary rules implicated by GenAI deployment.22

Since then, roughly half of U.S. state bars have issued formal ethics opinions or guidance. The jurisdictional landscape includes California (practical guidance, November 2023), Florida (Opinion 24-1, January 2024), New York City Bar (Opinion 24-05, July 2024), DC Bar (Opinion 388, April 2024), Pennsylvania and Philadelphia Bars (Joint Formal Opinion 2024-200, May 2024), and numerous others. While the specific requirements vary by jurisdiction, the shared structural consensus is clear: lawyers must understand how AI tools handle client data, cannot use tools that create unreasonable confidentiality risks, and must supervise AI-generated work product with the same diligence applied to associate-drafted work.2324

The privilege dimension adds a distinct layer of risk. Attorney-client privilege can be waived by disclosure to a non-privileged third party — and whether routing data through a vendor's AI system constitutes such a disclosure depends on the system architecture, the vendor's data handling practices, and whether a court views the AI as an extension of the attorney-client relationship or as an independent third party. In February 2026, Judge Jed Rakoff of the Southern District of New York ruled in United States v. Heppner that documents a criminal defendant created through exchanges with a publicly available AI tool were not shielded by attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine. While that ruling addressed a specific factual context, it represents the first judicial decision directly addressing privilege implications of consumer-grade AI platforms, and the direction of travel is clear: privilege protection does not automatically extend to interactions with public-facing AI systems.25262728

Decision Criteria: What Actually Determines the Right Architecture

With the regulatory landscape established, the substantive question is how to structure the build-vs-buy analysis. Four criteria are analytically primary; two are secondary but necessary inputs.

Primary Criteria

1. Data sensitivity and the "enclave threshold"

The most determinative variable is not the AI tool itself — it is the data the tool must process to perform its function. Some data categories carry regulatory or fiduciary constraints that effectively prohibit routing through systems whose data handling cannot be verified, regardless of vendor contractual assurances.

These categories are not merely sensitive — they are categories where the act of transmission itself may constitute a legal violation or a waiver of protected status:

  • Client-privileged material and attorney work product (law firms): routing through any system where a vendor or third party can access the data creates privilege waiver risk2725
  • Material non-public information (MNPI): any AI system that processes MNPI must be architected to prevent information barrier breaches; consumer-grade or shared-inference systems cannot provide the access controls this requires29
  • Nonpublic personal information (NPI) under GLBA: the Safeguards Rule requires vendor oversight and access controls that many off-the-shelf AI APIs cannot satisfy11
  • Proprietary trading strategies and algorithmic parameters: competitive sensitivity and potential information leakage through model training create dual risk
  • Regulated health and life insurance pricing data in the EU: high-risk classification under the AI Act Annex III requires documented human oversight and logging that must be verifiable, not simply contractually promised2021

For any use case requiring these data categories, the threshold question is not "has the vendor signed a data processing agreement" but rather "can we verify, at the technical architecture level, that data does not leave our controlled environment." Confidential computing — trusted execution environments (TEEs) that encrypt data in use as well as at rest and in transit — represents the emerging technical bridge between SaaS convenience and the data sovereignty that regulated institutions require. But even with TEEs, institutional verification of implementation and cryptographic attestation is required; vendor claims of enclave-grade security without attestable proof do not satisfy regulatory-level assurance.3031

2. Auditability and explainability requirements

Regulatory AI governance frameworks uniformly require that institutions be able to explain how a system reached a decision and reconstruct the decision pathway in response to regulatory inquiry. The FS AI RMF's MEASURE function explicitly requires explainability assessment for high-tier systems, recording how system behavior is understood and what compensating controls exist where it cannot be fully inspected. The EU AI Act requires comprehensive traceability documentation — training data, testing protocols, decision logs — for high-risk systems.3233

The problem with generic commercial AI is that explainability is typically incompatible with the system's business model. Foundation model providers do not share training data composition, model weights, or internal decision pathways. The Treasury's March 2024 cybersecurity risk report identified explainability of black-box AI solutions as a major gap, noting that the sector "would benefit from additional research and development on explainability solutions." Until those solutions exist at the commercial API level, institutions that need demonstrable explainability for regulatory purposes face a structural choice: use interpretable or auditable systems, or build the interpretability layer themselves.34

For use cases where explainability is a regulatory prerequisite — credit decisions subject to adverse action notice requirements under ECOA and Regulation B, high-risk AI under the EU AI Act, any AI model used in a credit underwriting context — relying on a black-box vendor without the ability to inspect and explain decision pathways creates examination exposure that no contract can cure.

3. Regulatory exposure of the use case

Not all AI use cases carry equal regulatory weight, and the governance architecture should be calibrated accordingly. The revised interagency model risk guidance (SR 26-2) establishes a materiality-based framework: model purpose, together with model exposure, determines the rigor of oversight required. The same logic applies to AI governance more broadly.4

A useful taxonomy for assessing regulatory exposure:

Use Case CategoryRegulatory ExposurePrimary Risks
Credit underwriting / scoringVery highFair lending, adverse action, EU AI Act high-risk
Customer-facing communicationsHighFINRA supervision, Rule 2210, Reg BI
AML / fraud surveillanceHighBSA compliance, model accuracy, audit trail
Legal research / document draftingHigh (privilege dimension)Confidentiality, competence, work product
Internal process automationModerateData handling, recordkeeping if output used
Productivity / coding assistanceLowerData leakage, information barriers
Research summarization (public data)LowHallucination risk, factual accuracy

The higher the regulatory exposure of the use case, the more the governance architecture needs to support active auditability, data segregation, and documented human oversight — capabilities that generic third-party tools typically cannot provide.

4. Vendor risk and concentration

The 2026 FINRA Regulatory Oversight Report flagged third-party risk as a central concern, noting that firms must maintain supervisory oversight of outsourced activities and conduct ongoing due diligence of vendor-supported systems. The GARP analysis of agentic AI and SR 11-7 identified third-party concentration — heavy reliance on a small number of external AI vendors and foundational model providers, many operating outside traditional regulatory perimeters — as amplifying systemic risk beyond individual firm-level controls.162

For compliance and risk leaders, third-party risk in AI has dimensions that traditional vendor risk management did not contemplate: training data supply chain, model drift and parameter updates that occur without formal redevelopment events, inference infrastructure shared across clients, and vendor business continuity in a concentrated market. When assessing an AI vendor, the relevant questions include: Can the vendor provide technical documentation sufficient to support EU AI Act Annex XII downstream information requirements? Does the data processing agreement prevent model training on client inputs? Can the vendor support the audit evidence required by the institution's model risk or AI governance program? What is the contractual right to obtain institution data in the event of vendor insolvency or acquisition?

Secondary Criteria

Speed-to-deployment and total cost of ownership remain real inputs, particularly for institutions operating at scale. Building a capable AI system from a foundation model typically requires specialized ML engineering talent, a production-grade MLOps infrastructure, and ongoing maintenance — with an initial build cycle of 12 to 18 months before production deployment. Off-the-shelf vendor tools can deploy in weeks. For institutions without significant AI engineering capacity, or for use cases with lower regulatory exposure, the cost of building internal capability may substantially exceed the benefit, particularly given the rapid pace of model improvement in the commercial market.35

The Treasury's March 2024 report explicitly noted the widening capability gap between large and small financial institutions, with large firms developing proprietary AI systems while smaller institutions lack the internal data resources and expertise to do the same. That gap is real and not likely to close in the near term. It argues for a risk-stratified approach that does not assume all institutions can or should build in-house.34

The Use Case Map: Where Each Side Wins, and Where a Third Option Applies

Applying the four primary criteria consistently produces three categories.

Category 1: Buy with appropriate vendor diligence

These use cases involve lower regulatory exposure, do not require access to enclave-threshold data, and are not the institution's primary competitive differentiator in a way that would make proprietary capability strategically essential.

Examples:

  • Internal productivity tools: drafting assistance, meeting summarization, code generation for non-production purposes
  • Research synthesis on public or non-privileged sources
  • IT operations, internal helpdesk, or HR knowledge base automation
  • Basic document classification where inputs are non-sensitive
  • Marketing content generation reviewed before publication

Governance requirements for bought tools in this category: The institution still needs a vendor due diligence process, a written AI use policy that covers these tools, access controls that prevent employees from inadvertently routing sensitive data through tools approved only for non-sensitive use, and a monitoring process. FINRA's expectation that written supervisory procedures be "appropriately tailored" to the firm's evolving use of technology applies even to lower-risk tools. The point is not that governance is unnecessary — it is that full model-risk-program-level governance and proprietary deployment infrastructure are disproportionate to the risk.14

Category 2: Build in-house, or deploy via private/enclaved architecture

These use cases involve one or more of the following: enclave-threshold data categories, regulatory requirements for explainability or auditability that generic vendors cannot satisfy, or regulatory exposure so high that the institution must be able to demonstrate governance to an examiner using verifiable evidence rather than vendor attestations.

Examples:

  • Credit underwriting and scoring models for consumer or commercial lending
  • AML transaction monitoring and suspicious activity detection
  • Client portfolio management systems with personalized recommendations (Reg BI / fiduciary context)
  • Any system processing client-privileged communications in a law firm
  • Investment banking deal management tools touching MNPI
  • High-risk EU AI Act use cases (creditworthiness evaluation, insurance pricing)
  • Proprietary trading strategy analysis or algorithm development

"In-house" does not necessarily mean "on-premises." Private cloud deployment, dedicated tenant environments with contractually segregated infrastructure, and confidential computing architectures using TEEs can provide equivalent data control with reduced infrastructure burden. The criterion is not physical location — it is verifiable data sovereignty: the institution must be able to confirm, through technical means rather than contractual promises alone, that data does not flow to parties it should not reach and that the processing environment meets the institution's security and audit requirements.30

For law firms specifically, the privilege analysis does not yet yield clean answers about whether enterprise-tier deployments of commercial models adequately protect privileged material. ABA Formal Opinion 512 requires lawyers to understand the data handling practices of any GenAI tool they use. Multiple state bar opinions — including Florida Opinion 24-1 and Pennsylvania/Philadelphia Joint Opinion 2024-200 — have emphasized that even closed, firm-internal self-learning AI systems create risk that one client's information may be applied to another client's matter. A firm-internal deployment trained on the firm's own matter data is not automatically safe; the architecture must be designed to prevent cross-matter information leakage, typically through client-matter-level access controls and non-self-learning inference configurations.3622

Category 3: Hybrid architectures — the "buy-and-control" middle

This category captures a large and growing portion of practical AI deployment: use cases where a commercial foundation model provides the core capability, but the institution retains control over the deployment architecture, the data pipeline, and the inference environment.

Examples:

  • A large commercial LLM deployed on the institution's private cloud or dedicated tenant, with no data leaving the perimeter
  • A retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system built on proprietary documents, where the model is external but the data layer is internal and controlled
  • A regulatory reporting drafting tool that uses a commercial model for prose generation but restricts inputs to pre-processed, non-sensitive data representations
  • A compliance monitoring tool where a vendor model is fine-tuned on the institution's data but deployed in an environment the institution controls

This architecture is increasingly common because it separates the capability layer (where commercial models provide genuine advantages in scale and investment) from the data control layer (where institutional requirements are non-negotiable). Its governance requirements, however, are closer to Category 2 than Category 1: the institution must still be able to audit the system's outputs, demonstrate data control, and satisfy vendor oversight requirements under the interagency third-party risk guidance. The vendor still provides the foundation model, and the institution must obtain adequate technical documentation from that vendor — including under Article 53 of the EU AI Act for EU-facing deployments.920

A Practical Decision Framework

Translating the above into an operational tool, institutions can assess each proposed AI use case against five threshold questions. A single "no" to any of the first three questions should override considerations of cost and speed.

Threshold questions (any "no" signals Category 2 or 3):

  1. Can this use case be performed without processing enclave-threshold data? (Client-privileged material, MNPI, NPI under GLBA, EU high-risk AI input data)
  1. If the system produces a decision that affects a customer, counterparty, or regulated activity, can the institution explain and reconstruct that decision using evidence it controls — not evidence held by the vendor?
  1. Can the institution satisfy its vendor oversight obligations under applicable third-party risk management guidance by reviewing this vendor's technical documentation, testing the system's performance on its own data, and maintaining effective ongoing monitoring?

Calibration questions (inform tier and governance intensity):

  1. What is the regulatory exposure of this use case? (See the taxonomy above — higher exposure requires more governance infrastructure regardless of deployment choice.)
  1. What is the institution's AI engineering capacity and timeline? (Capacity constraints are real; they affect sequencing and architecture choices but should not override threshold constraints.)

If a use case passes all three threshold questions, a commercial off-the-shelf tool with appropriate vendor diligence and an AI governance program calibrated to its risk level is defensible. If a use case fails any of the threshold questions, the institution must either shift to a Category 2 or 3 architecture, redesign the use case to eliminate the disqualifying element, or not deploy the use case.

Where the Decision Remains Genuinely Hard

The framework above establishes the clear cases on each side. The genuinely contested ground — where reasonable institutions are making different choices and where regulatory guidance has not yet converged — deserves honest acknowledgment.

GenAI and the model governance gap

The explicit exclusion of generative and agentic AI from the scope of SR 26-2 was intended to reduce compliance burden by not forcing GenAI tools into a model risk validation framework designed for statistical credit models. In practice, it has created a governance gap that institutions are filling inconsistently. The agencies indicated they plan to issue a request for information on AI governance, including GenAI and agentic AI. Until that guidance exists, institutions face genuine uncertainty about what "appropriate governance" for excluded systems actually looks like in examination practice — and examiners retain discretion to assess whether governance is "sound" even without a formal framework.54

Enterprise commercial tiers and their actual security claims

Major AI providers now offer enterprise tiers with commitments not to train on customer inputs, with dedicated infrastructure options, and with data processing agreements. Whether these arrangements actually satisfy the legal and regulatory requirements applicable to a specific institution depends on a fact-intensive analysis that the market has not uniformly resolved. The Bloomberg Law reporting on privilege risks and the Heppner ruling both illustrate that vendor assurances and contractual terms do not definitively determine the legal analysis — courts and regulators may reach independent conclusions about whether data was adequately protected. Institutions making risk-based decisions to use enterprise commercial tiers for moderately sensitive use cases are taking a bet that is not without basis, but is also not without residual risk.2825

Agentic AI and the breakdown of traditional governance assumptions

The GARP analysis of SR 11-7 in the age of agentic AI identified a structural problem that applies equally to the build-vs-buy question: governance frameworks designed for periodic validation of stable systems are not designed for systems that recalibrate autonomously or initiate actions in real time. FINRA's 2026 report flagged autonomous AI agents as presenting unique risks including autonomy without human validation, actions beyond intended scope, and challenges in auditability. Whether an institution builds or buys an agentic AI system, the governance mechanisms required for that system are substantially more demanding than for conventional AI tools, and the regulatory guidance on what those mechanisms should look like does not yet exist in final form.132

The law firm calculus

Law firms face a distinct version of the problem because the relevant regulatory bodies — state bars — are not uniform, are still issuing guidance, and have produced opinions that establish the principle of required vendor diligence without always specifying what adequate diligence looks like in practice. The direction of travel — toward requiring demonstrable data controls, explicit confidentiality protections, and architectural separation from training pipelines — is clear. But the precise line between an acceptable enterprise deployment and an unacceptable one remains the subject of ongoing opinion development, and firms in multiple jurisdictions must track an evolving patchwork rather than a single rule. The Heppner ruling adds a litigation-specific dimension that extends the risk analysis beyond ethics compliance into the actual risk of privilege waiver in contested proceedings.223628

A Note on What "Regulated" Actually Requires

Throughout this analysis, the recurring theme is that regulated status creates obligations that cannot be contractually transferred. A vendor can promise confidentiality. A vendor cannot accept regulatory accountability for your ECOA adverse action notices, your FINRA supervisory procedures, your Model Rule 1.6 obligations, or your EU AI Act high-risk system conformity assessment. Those obligations belong to the institution, they are enforced against the institution, and they must be discharged by the institution.

That framing does not imply that institutions should build everything — that would be neither practical nor necessary. It implies that the governance analysis must precede the procurement decision, that deployment architecture must be chosen with regulatory obligations as binding constraints rather than considerations, and that "the vendor handles it" is not an answer that satisfies a regulator, a court, or an ethics committee.

The institutions that navigate this well are not the ones that build the most or buy the most. They are the ones that have done the categorization work — understanding what each use case requires, which data it touches, and what governance the applicable regulatory framework demands — before they sign a contract or write a line of model code.
