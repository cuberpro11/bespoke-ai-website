Knowledge management that lawyers actually use

A candid look at why legal KM investment fails at the retrieval layer, and what changes when the knowledge comes to the lawyer, not the other way around.

Q4 202516 MIN

There is a version of this article that opens with a congratulatory overview of how far legal knowledge management has come, the investments made, the libraries built, the taxonomies lovingly designed. That version would be misleading.

The honest version starts here: most lawyers, most of the time, do not search the precedent database. They ask a colleague, reuse the last document they worked on, or draft from memory. The knowledge management system sits at the end of a browser bookmark, behind a login, waiting. It has been waiting for years. This is not a fringe problem at poorly resourced firms. It is the modal behavior across the industry, and it persists regardless of how good the content inside the system actually is.

The reason is retrieval friction. Not bad content. Friction.

The Retrieval Friction Problem

The economics of lawyering are brutally simple: every minute spent on a non-billable task is a minute that is simultaneously lost to revenue and must be explained to a time-tracking system. In that environment, leaving the drafting environment to search a separate repository is not a neutral act — it is a deliberate interruption of flow that requires a lawyer to decide, in real time, that a search is worth the interruption cost. That decision, made dozens of times a day, usually resolves the same way: don't search. Use what's at hand.

The evidence for this pattern is diffuse but consistent. An IDC whitepaper, cited widely in the legal technology literature, found that information workers — a category that includes lawyers — spend approximately 2.3 hours per week searching for, but not finding, the right documents, and another 2.0 hours recreating documents they could not locate. That is not a failure of the documents to exist; it is a failure of retrieval. The same data suggested this wasted time cost firms roughly $9,071 per lawyer per year in lost productivity. More recent estimates put the figure higher — one analysis of law firm inefficiencies cited costs of up to $19,732 per knowledge worker annually when all document management friction was aggregated.12

Bloomberg Law's 2024 workload survey found that attorneys clock approximately 48 hours per week but bill only 36, a 12-hour gap attributed substantially to administrative overhead and document navigation. Clio's Legal Trends data, meanwhile, has consistently shown lawyers spending only around 2.3 to 2.5 hours per day on billable tasks — less than a third of the working day — with administrative tasks consuming the largest share of the remainder. Neither survey isolates KM search specifically, but the picture they collectively paint is of a profession already working at maximum cognitive load, in which any additional friction in the retrieval environment will simply cause the retrieval to be skipped.345

The Thomson Reuters Institute's 2024 Legal Department Operations (LDO) Index, which surveyed corporate legal departments, found that knowledge management tools — along with document and process automation — fell into the category respondents most often described as underutilized rather than valuable. This is a striking framing: respondents did not say these tools were bad or useless. They said they were underutilized. The investment was made; the behavior did not follow.6

The Documented Failure Modes

KM system underutilization in law firms is not mysterious. The failure modes are well-documented, consistent across firm types and sizes, and have been consistent for long enough that the industry has had ample time not to fix them. They cluster into four areas.

The Metadata Burden

Most legal KM systems depend, at least partially, on human-applied metadata to make content findable. Tagging a document with the right matter type, jurisdiction, clause type, and practice area makes it surfaceable; not tagging it makes it functionally invisible. The problem is that the person best positioned to tag the document correctly — the lawyer who just produced it — is also the person with the least incentive to do so. The work is billed; the tagging is overhead.

The result is predictable and consistent. A metadata design analysis for legal information architecture observes that "lawyers are unlikely to complete a long list of metadata fields when saving documents" and that "the more effort required, the lower the data quality". Common failure patterns include tagging fatigue (incomplete fields), lack of ownership (no one responsible for maintaining standards), and over-engineering (hundreds of matter types that users cannot distinguish). When the taxonomy is ignored, the database fills with content that cannot reliably be retrieved, which reduces lawyer trust in results, which further reduces their willingness to search, which makes the investment in tagging infrastructure look even more pointless.7

One industry analysis attributed roughly 27% of law firm KM failures to poor metadata and tagging — content incorrectly categorized or categorized not at all. Another noted that siloed repositories (search limited to a single database, not federated across the firm's document management landscape) accounted for a further 19%. These numbers should be treated as illustrative rather than authoritative, but they point in a direction that is consistent with qualitative commentary across the field.8

The Consensus Problem

Even setting aside retrieval infrastructure, legal KM faces an upstream problem: the content itself is often contested. Law firms do not naturally converge on shared precedents. Partners have personal preferences. Practice groups have local variations. Work produced at previous firms travels with laterals and gets folded into personal document folders, neither formally adopted nor formally rejected.

A practitioner-facing series on Slaw.ca — one of the more practically honest sources in the Canadian legal technology literature — identified the core difficulty plainly: "different lawyers often have varying ways of approaching and presenting the same content, and there is a natural tendency to prefer what one knows best". The piece notes that getting buy-in for shared precedents requires overcoming deeply ingrained individual ownership behaviors, and that without buy-in, the precedent collection will be bypassed by precisely the people it was built for.9

This pattern is observable in how firms actually draft. When asked why they had not automated document production, firms frequently claim they "have no precedents" — but what they mean is they have no agreed firm-wide precedents. In reality, lawyers are drafting constantly. What they are using is a personal folder, a downloaded template from a commercial provider, a document received from counterparty counsel on a previous deal. The firm's institutional knowledge is in production, but it is fragmented, ungoverned, and invisible to the KM function.10

An honest commentary on Slaw notes the same dynamic from a different angle: "In reality, the law firm precedent can be a costly expense that, at the end of the day, is not utilized. Indeed, senior lawyers will thwart the efforts of firms to standardize and systemize agreements, and advise young lawyers to avoid using these systems". This is the version of the adoption problem that tends not to appear in vendor case studies.11

The Interface Problem

Many legal KM systems are old. Not old as in last year's release — old as in architecturally and experientially antiquated. An analysis in Legal 500's technology commentary describes the persistent reality: "Knowledge is still delivered via old search engines, intranets, practice area pages, and knowhow collections, often on old versions of tools like SharePoint. The interfaces are cumbersome and antiquated, lacking ease of use and providing limited navigation that modern applications offer". The comparison drawn is to consumer search. Think Google, the piece suggests — but what many firms have is something closer to a 2005-era intranet with a keyword search box.12

The search relevance problem compounds the interface problem. Legacy DMS search is keyword-based; it returns documents containing the searched terms, not documents that are conceptually relevant to the task at hand. A lawyer searching for a warranty limitation clause in a SaaS agreement might search "warranty" and receive hundreds of documents across every practice area, undifferentiated by deal type, jurisdiction, or negotiated outcome. The results are noisy; the signal is buried. The lawyer defaults to asking a colleague who worked on the last similar deal.

Eighty percent of legal data is unstructured — emails, PDFs, Word documents — stored in its original format without structural enrichment. Of this, by one industry estimate, 42% is never reused at all: produced once, billed once, and then effectively archived in a subfolder that will never surface in a search. This is not a storage problem. It is a retrieval design problem.1314

The Workflow Separation Problem

This is the failure mode that subsumes all the others, and the one that the industry has been slowest to name clearly. A knowledge management system that lives in a separate window, behind a separate login, requiring a separate search workflow, will always be competing with the path of least resistance: staying in the document.

Legal drafting happens almost universally in Microsoft Word. This is not going to change in the near term. Any KM tool that requires a lawyer to leave Word, navigate to a portal, formulate a query, evaluate results, copy relevant language, and return to the draft has inserted six discrete friction points between the lawyer and the knowledge. Each friction point is a decision gate at which the lawyer can — and frequently does — choose to proceed without the retrieved information.

The legal tech commentary is unusually blunt about this. One practitioner-facing analysis lists "forcing lawyers to leave Word" as the first and primary common pitfall in legal tech adoption, noting simply that "if a new tool requires users to abandon Microsoft Word, adoption is likely to fail" and that "Word is still a lawyer's preferred place for legal drafting". The fix is not better marketing or more training — the fix is integration.15

What Changes When KM Moves Into the Drafting Flow

The body of evidence on embedded or in-flow KM is still forming, but the directional signal is consistent enough to be useful. When relevant knowledge is surfaced inside the drafting environment — in a Word sidebar, triggered by the content being drafted, without requiring an explicit search — the behavioral pattern changes. Not because the underlying knowledge improved, but because the friction of accessing it collapsed.

The mechanism is what behavioral scientists would recognize as a default intervention. Changing the default from "no knowledge surfaced" to "relevant knowledge surfaced automatically" captures lawyers who would never have chosen to search but will use information that appears at their elbow. The decision cost drops to near zero.

Litera, whose platform serves the large majority of the AmLaw 200, has articulated this model as "embedded AI" — one that "treats the existing Microsoft 365 environment as the foundation for legal AI adoption" and delivers capability "inside the tools lawyers already use daily". Their published analysis describes a specific failure pattern: lawyers defaulting to Excel spreadsheets and email threads they have used for years when new tools require context-switching, observing that "work filed in personal Outlook folders rather than the DMS" is precisely the pattern that "pushes institutional knowledge outside any system that can retrieve or govern it". Their framing of "opportunity minutes" — time returned to lawyers at the workflow level — is a practical way of talking about what KM adoption actually requires: not compelling content, but zero-friction access to it at the moment it is needed.16

DraftWise, which integrates directly into Microsoft Word and indexes a firm's prior deal history for clause-level retrieval, represents one of the clearer instances of the embedded model applied to transactional drafting. Ropes & Gray's implementation, announced in late 2025, was described as eliminating "the need for tedious searches through precedent libraries or the reconstruction of legal clauses" by surfacing firm-specific precedents directly within Word as lawyers draft. The DraftWise general counsel's published account of the experience is more candid than most: "I no longer have to toggle between folders, email chains, and executed agreements to confirm how we did something before. Our negotiated precedent appears directly in the Word sidebar when it is relevant". That observation about the Word sidebar is doing real explanatory work. The knowledge did not change. Its physical location relative to the work did.1718

Gunderson Dettmer, an early DraftWise design partner, and Orrick have each noted the platform's relevance to integrating AI into their practice group playbooks and workflows. These are not claims about accuracy improvements or content richness; they are observations about workflow fit. The tool meets lawyers where the work already is.19

The LEXICON framework for legal KM engagement, published by Lucidea and aimed at practitioners running KM functions, puts the design principle plainly as its first item: "KM cannot be an extra step; it must be embedded into any workflow. Automating information capture, embedding precedents in templates, and surfacing know-how directly in drafting tools makes knowledge easy to reuse and ensures that it supports billable activity rather than competing with it". That last clause — competing with it — is the correct way to understand what a separate-portal KM system is doing. It is asking lawyers to divert time from billable work to retrieve institutional knowledge. The rational response is to not divert.20

The Grounding Dimension

In-flow retrieval also addresses a trust problem that standalone AI tools have been unable to resolve. When a lawyer queries a generic AI tool, the output is ungrounded — it reflects no specific precedent, no firm-specific negotiating position, no client-specific history. Lawyers are trained skeptics; ungrounded output requires independent verification, which restores most or all of the time that was supposed to be saved.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architectures address this by grounding AI outputs in retrieved source material — specific clauses from prior agreements, specific positions from the firm's negotiating history. When the lawyer can see that a suggested clause came from a prior firm precedent, with a citation to the source document, the verification burden drops significantly. The output is traceable; its provenance is visible. That transparency is doing adoption work that better search alone cannot do.21

A six-month pilot study by the Municipality of Amsterdam, deploying a RAG-based drafting assistance system (LegalCheck) for legal advice letters, found that jurists retained approximately 81% of AI-generated content verbatim in their final letters, with only 8% new content added. More practically, jurists reported a 50–70% reduction in total case handling time for straightforward cases, with the drafting stage alone showing a 92% time saving. Initial skepticism was "largely overcome due to consistent quality, source traceability, and human control over final approval". This last element is worth noting: trust in the system grew not because the AI improved, but because the system showed its work. The cited sources were visible; the audit trail was present. Lawyers could verify without reconstructing from scratch.22

This is not a commercial legal AI deployment — it is a public sector pilot — but the behavioral dynamics translate. When grounded retrieval is embedded in the drafting workflow, and when outputs come with source citations that lawyers can check, the combination of proximity and traceability produces the adoption behavior that better portal search could not achieve.

What Has to Be True Before Any of This Works

Embedding retrieval in the drafting flow does not bypass the underlying content problem described earlier — it raises the cost of ignoring it. A sidebar that surfaces the wrong clause, or a duplicate of a clause with three abandoned variants sitting alongside it, converts the metadata and consensus failures from Section One into visible, moment-of-drafting friction rather than background rot. Grounded retrieval makes bad precedent hygiene more damaging, not less, because the lawyer now sees the mess at the exact point they are deciding whether to trust the system.

Three prerequisites do most of the work. First, deduplication and version control on the underlying corpus: a retrieval system indexing five negotiated variants of the same clause with no signal about which is current, which was superseded, and which was a one-off concession for a difficult counterparty will surface all five with equal confidence, reproducing the fragmentation problem inside the tool meant to solve it. Second, a narrowed and enforced taxonomy — the LEXICON framework's embedding principle only functions if what gets embedded is worth surfacing, which means the matter-type and clause-type categories need to be few enough that classification is unambiguous, not the "hundreds of matter types" over-engineering the metadata research above identified as a failure pattern in its own right. Third, and least technical: someone has to own curation on an ongoing basis — a named function with the authority to retire superseded language and resolve competing partner preferences, because a self-curating corpus is not something document volume produces on its own.

None of this is a prerequisite in the sense of a multi-year data project that has to finish before a pilot can start. Firms that have run embedded-retrieval pilots successfully tend to start narrow — one practice group, one high-volume agreement type — precisely so the corpus is small enough to curate by hand before scaling the workflow pattern to the rest of the firm. The mistake is treating corpus quality as a concern that in-flow retrieval makes obsolete, when it is closer to the opposite: proximity is what finally makes precedent hygiene consequential enough that firms have a reason to do it.

Proximity Is the Deciding Factor, Not Search Quality

It would be satisfying if the solution to legal KM adoption were simply better search — more semantic, more contextually aware, less dependent on keyword matching. Better search would mean the problem was technical, and technical problems can be purchased. But the evidence suggests that search quality is a secondary variable, not a primary one.

The primary variable is proximity: how far is the knowledge from the work?

The ILTA 2025 Technology Survey, drawing on responses from 580 law firms representing 152,000 attorneys, found that 57% of respondents identified resistance to change as the single biggest barrier to adopting emerging technologies — up from 54% in 2024. Security and risk concerns came second at 54%. "Resistance to change" is a frustrating finding because it sounds like a cultural problem requiring cultural solutions, but it is more useful understood as a behavioral problem requiring workflow solutions. Resistance to change, in a legal context, mostly means lawyers continuing to do what they were doing before the new tool arrived. The new tool failed to create a better default.2324

A McKinsey framing cited in legal tech commentary is pertinent here: 70% of all organizational change initiatives fail due to employee resistance and lack of change management. That figure applies as broadly to legal KM implementations as anywhere else. The difference between a KM initiative that gets ignored and one that gets used is not usually the quality of the content — it is whether using the knowledge requires active behavior change, or whether it simply shows up.25

When KM lives in a separate destination, using it requires active behavior change. Lawyers must decide to leave, search, evaluate, copy, and return. Most do not. When KM is embedded in the drafting tool, using it is the default. The knowledge appears; the lawyer decides whether to use it. That is a fundamentally different decision, and it is one that lawyers make differently.

The Legal 500 analysis of KM design articulated the necessary shift: away from "the traditional content-led methodology of 'retrieve and read' towards a more fluid, intuitive and data-driven way of surfacing information" so that "content is created in a way that fits in with how lawyers perform tasks, appearing dynamically at the point of need within their workflows". This is not a new observation in legal KM circles. What is new is that the technical capability to deliver it — semantic search, RAG architectures, Word-native AI sidebars — now exists at enterprise scale.12

The uncomfortable implication is that the question of whether a KM system is used has less to do with investment in content curation and more to do with investment in workflow integration. Firms that have spent years building authoritative, well-maintained precedent libraries may have created excellent raw material that is effectively inaccessible because it lives in the wrong place. The library is not the limiting constraint. The trip to the library is.

A Closing Observation

Legal knowledge management has spent thirty years trying to solve an adoption problem by improving the library. The libraries are often genuinely good. The problem was never the library.

The lawyers who do not use the KM system are not making an irrational choice. They are making a perfectly rational one: the expected value of interrupting their workflow to search a separate portal is usually lower than the expected value of proceeding with what is at hand. That calculation changes only when the knowledge is at hand — when it surfaces in the sidebar without requiring a deliberate retrieval decision.

Firms that have recognized this are not abandoning their KM investment. They are changing its delivery architecture. The rest continue to fund excellent databases that nobody searches, and to diagnose the problem as a training issue, or a culture issue, or a change management issue, when the actual issue is simpler and harder to admit: the system requires the lawyer to come to it, and lawyers, as a rule, do not come.
